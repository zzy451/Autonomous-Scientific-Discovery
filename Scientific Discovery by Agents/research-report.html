A field report · May 2026

AI Agents, Skills, and the Academic Workflow

Somewhere between the 2024 prompt-engineering era and the late-2026 papers where agents write their own papers, a new object quietly took over scientific tooling: the skill. This is a tour of where that object came from, what it can do today, how we measure it, and why autonomous discovery now needs four hard-gated cognitive postures rather than one general-purpose assistant persona.

By the editors · Reading time ~24 min · 2026-05-13

IA hinge point

Two years that changed what "using AI to do research" means.

In August 2024, a system called AI Scientist submitted a paper to a real machine-learning workshop and got it accepted. Nobody on the program committee was told, at first, that it had been written end-to-end by an autonomous agent. The paper was not a masterpiece, but it was plausible: it proposed a hypothesis, ran experiments, plotted results, and assembled a draft. A year and a half later, in January 2026, Nature published the technical follow-up, making it the first time a journal of that caliber treated "the researcher was an agent" as a first-class methodological fact.

Two months before that publication, OpenAI had quietly shipped PaperBench: a benchmark in which an agent must reproduce twenty ICML-2024 Spotlight or Oral papers from scratch, graded against 8,316 fine-grained rubric leaves that the original authors helped design. And in April 2026, a 38-author team from MIT Media Lab, Michigan, and Stanford posted a proposal to replace the paper itself — not the writing of papers, but the object of a paper — with something more amenable to machines. We will come back to that paper at length in Section V.

These are not isolated events. They sit on top of a much quieter change that has happened in developer tooling over roughly eighteen months: the emergence of the skill as the basic unit of agent capability. A skill is not a model, not a prompt, not a plugin in the 2023 sense; it is a small, self-contained procedure that an AI agent can discover, read, and invoke on its own. There are now tens of thousands of them. They are, cumulatively, what makes the PaperBench and AI-Scientist stories possible — and they are what the rest of this essay is about.

IIWhat a skill is, actually

Before the survey, a definition — because the word is often used loosely.

An agent skill is a folder on disk. Inside that folder is a single Markdown file, usually called SKILL.md, with a short YAML header that states the skill's name, what it is for, and the conditions under which an agent should reach for it. Below the header is prose: a procedure, a checklist, sometimes a small script, always written to be read by a language model rather than a human. Supporting material — reference pages, templates, helper code — sits beside it in optional sub-folders.

That sounds modest, and it is. The point is what the modesty buys. A skill is discoverable — the agent finds it without being told. It is composable — one skill can call another without coordination from a human. It is portable — the same folder works in Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, or Gemini CLI because they have converged on the same on-disk format. And it is legible — a PhD student can read the Markdown and understand what the agent is supposed to do, which is the precondition for trusting the agent to do it.

Compared with a prompt, a skill is durable: it lives in a file rather than a chat window, which means it can be versioned, reviewed, and cited. Compared with a plugin in the 2023 ChatGPT sense, it is cheap: no API registration, no OAuth dance, no publisher review. The barrier between "I have a procedure that works" and "my agent now knows this procedure" has collapsed to the act of writing a Markdown file. That collapse is why the ecosystem grew so fast — and the shape of that ecosystem is the next thing worth looking at.

IIIThe ecosystem, seen from 30,000 feet

Who builds skills, where they live, and how to read the topology.

If you search GitHub for repositories tagged claude-skills or agent-skills, you will find the current ecosystem resolves into four distinct layers. It is worth naming them, because they have different purposes, different audiences, and very different half-lives.

1. The reference implementations

At the bottom sit the model-lab repositories — anthropics/skills (131k stars) and the corresponding official plugin directory. These set the format, settle debates about the YAML schema, and ship the document-processing primitives (PDF, DOCX, XLSX, PPTX) that almost every downstream skill relies on.

2. The domain collections

Above them sit focused collections built by practitioners — labs, journals, and individual researchers who codify their own methodology. In academia, the most notable are:

The cadence at which this layer is moving is perhaps the single clearest signal that the field has entered a new phase. academic-research-skills, the four-skill pipeline in the table above, is a useful case in point: opened on 26 February 2026 by the independent developer Cheng-I Wu (吴政宜), it crossed 6,000 stars in roughly seventy-five days, trending on Hacker News along the way, and shipped its seventh major release (v3.7.0) in early May. Ten years ago, a solo author shipping methodology of this quality would have needed a tenured appointment and a graduate student to maintain it. The distribution graph has quietly flattened: a weekend, a SKILL.md, and a readable repository are now enough for a piece of research infrastructure to acquire real users.

3. The autonomous research systems

A parallel thread does not ship skills at all — it ships entire pipelines. The best-known are Sakana's AI Scientist (13.5k ⭐) and its v2, gpt-researcher (27k ⭐), Agent Laboratory, and HKU's AI-Researcher (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight). These are the systems that take a research question and return a draft paper — and whose numbers set the ceiling against which the skill-based approach must eventually be measured.

4. The meta-lists

Finally, a small number of curated catalogues track the whole landscape so you don't have to. Two are worth knowing: the community-run ComposioHQ / awesome-claude-skills (59k ⭐), which is the most comprehensive general-purpose index, and the Stanford REAP × CoPaper.AI list, which specialises in empirical social-science workflows and, as of its April 2026 update, indexes 119 repositories covering 23,000+ skills, organised by research stage.

A field with this many artifacts and this much movement needs benchmarks, or every claim of progress is unverifiable. The next section walks the benchmark map.

IVMeasuring progress

Twenty-nine public benchmarks, organised by the stage of research they evaluate.

A healthy field needs public benchmarks. For scientific agents, the last two years produced an unusually clean set of them — not because any single one is perfect, but because together they cover the whole research pipeline with enough overlap to triangulate. What follows is a condensed map. Every row has been cross-checked against OpenAlex for authorship and DOI; links go to the canonical preprint where one exists.

Browsing and general assistance

The earliest and best-known is GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023) — 450 real-world questions in three difficulty tiers. Humans score 92%; GPT-4 with plug-ins at release scored about 15%. GAIA is still the default entry point for anyone evaluating a research-assistant agent. Its successor papers are BrowseComp (OpenAI, 2025, 1,266 hard-to-find-information questions) and SimpleQA (OpenAI, 2024, 4,326 short-form factuality questions designed to surface hallucination). Humanity's Last Exam (CAIS + Scale, 2025) raises the ceiling with 3,000+ expert-grade questions across the sciences and humanities.

Machine-learning research as a task

Here the canonical three are MLAgentBench (Huang et al., ICML 2024: 13 Kaggle-style improvement tasks), MLE-Bench (OpenAI, 2024: 75 Kaggle competitions, scored by medal rate against human percentile), and RE-Bench (METR, 2024: seven AI-R&D tasks where the baseline is not a model but a human researcher on the same four-hour budget). Alongside them: ScienceAgentBench (OSU, ICLR'25: 102 scientific data-analysis tasks), SUPER (AI2, EMNLP 2024: reproducing research repositories), and CORE-Bench (2024: 90 computational reproducibility tasks).

Paper-level reproduction

This category is the bar for end-to-end competence. The dominant entry is PaperBench: given an ICML 2024 paper and its appendix, reproduce the work from scratch. Scoring is done against a rubric tree that the original authors helped design and that decomposes each paper into 8,316 evaluable leaves. It is the most honest benchmark in this space because the rubric was built by the people who know what the paper was supposed to prove. Paper2Code (2025) is a lighter-weight cousin focused on paper-to-runnable-code.

Literature review, idea generation, and peer review

The survey-writing task has a clear leader: AutoSurvey (NeurIPS 2024). Its four-dimensional rubric — citation recall, citation precision, content coverage, structural coherence — has quietly become the de facto metric for long-form scholarly generation.

For idea generation, the reference is a Stanford human study from NeurIPS 2024 (Si et al.), which asked 79 NLP researchers to blindly rate AI-generated vs. human-generated research ideas on novelty, excitement, feasibility, and effectiveness. The finding — that AI ideas score higher on novelty but lower on feasibility — is the closest thing the field has to a load-bearing empirical result. IdeaBench and AAAR-1.0 automate pieces of that.

For peer review, the historical anchor is PeerRead (NAACL 2018), still cited; the modern entries are ReviewerGPT (CMU, 2023), Reviewer2 (2024), and AgentReview (EMNLP 2024), which is particularly interesting because it introduces deliberately biased reviewers to test whether the multi-agent simulation reproduces the known pathologies of real conference review.

Figures and tables

ChartQA (ACL 2022) is still the canon for chart understanding; SciCap+ and Paper2Fig100k handle the caption-generation and figure-reconstruction tasks respectively.

Benchmarks let us measure — but measurement only explains what exists. The three sections that follow are the field’s answer to a different question: what, in 2026, should the underlying object even be? Each takes one face of it.

VThe architectural frontier · what a skill contains

A 38-author April 2026 paper that, if correct, changes not how papers are written but what a paper is.

In late April 2026 a team of 38 authors — led from MIT Media Lab, the University of Michigan, and Stanford, and including Alex Pentland — posted an arXiv preprint with the deliberately provocative title The Last Human-Written Paper: Agent-Native Research Artifacts. The claim is not that humans will stop writing papers. The claim is that the paper itself — the PDF, the narrative, the compressed linear argument — has been the wrong object for about two years, and nobody noticed because only humans were reading them.

Paper: arXiv:2604.24658 · Code: Orchestra-Research/Agent-Native-Research-Artifact · License: MIT

The diagnosis: two taxes

The authors' framing is precise. Publication compiles a branching, iterative research process into a linear narrative, and the compilation imposes two structural costs.

The storytelling tax is what the paper throws away to fit the genre — failed experiments, rejected hypotheses, the branching exploration process that actually produced the finding. These are discarded because they do not fit a linear argument, and their absence is invisible to a reader who already trusts the author.

The engineering tax is the gap between "enough prose for a reviewer" and "enough specification for a machine to reproduce the work". Authors write to a reader who can infer missing detail; agents cannot infer, so they rediscover every missing detail at cost.

The empirical case, in two numbers Only 45.4% of the 8,921 reproduction requirements extracted from 23 ICML 2024 papers are fully specified in the published PDFs. And on RE-Bench, failed agent runs account for 90.2% of total dollar cost across 24,008 runs, because each agent independently rediscovers the same dead ends. Tolerable when every reader was human; load-bearing waste when readers are machines.

The proposal: a four-layer artifact

In place of the PDF the authors propose an Agent-Native Research Artifact (ARA) — a directory structured in four interlocking layers, meant to be read and written by agents, though still legible to humans.

Three details make the protocol more than a filesystem convention. First, every claim in logic/ carries a forensic binding to the experiment that supports it, and every experiment binds to raw evidence in evidence/. Second, every entry carries a provenance tag — user, ai-suggested, ai-executed, or user-revised — distinguishing human-confirmed facts from AI inferences. Third, and most distinctively, dead ends are first-class citizens: the exploration tree preserves rejected alternatives and failed approaches alongside the surviving story, so future agents do not have to rediscover them.

A twenty-one-point jump on PaperBench question-answering is not subtle. It is the kind of number that usually means either the benchmark is broken or the intervention is real. The PaperBench rubric is, as discussed, unusually author-validated, which makes the second reading hard to dismiss.

The authors are refreshingly honest about the limit: on RE-Bench's five open-ended extension tasks, preserved failure traces can sometimes constrain a capable agent — locking it inside the box the previous run already explored, rather than letting it step outside. The benefit is strongly conditional on the agent's own strength. That caveat, placed in the abstract rather than buried in the appendix, tells you something about the paper's seriousness.

ARA tells us what a skill — or, more generally, a reusable research artifact — should contain if an agent is going to read it. It does not tell us how a skill comes into being in the first place. That is the question a second 2026 paper, posted from a different continent, set out to answer.

VIThe other frontier · how a skill is born

A second 2026 paper, posted from a UIUC–Google collaboration, runs orthogonal to the first. Where ARA reshapes the paper, SkillOS reshapes the skill.

Where ARA reshapes the paper, SkillOS (Ouyang et al., May 2026) reshapes the skill — or rather, it stops treating the skill as a thing a human writes and asks whether an agent can learn to curate its own skill library. The intervention sits one level closer to practice than ARA’s, and one level closer to policy. Neither paper cites the other; both, by May, were visibly operating on the same underlying question.

Paper: arXiv:2605.06614 · Sixteen authors led by Siru Ouyang (UIUC / Google Research); last author Chen-Yu Lee. Published 2026-05-07.

The setup

SkillOS factors an agent into two components. The executor is frozen — a capable LLM that, given a task, retrieves relevant skills from a repository and applies them. The curator is trainable: after each task, it decides what to add, revise, or remove from the repository based on what just happened. The repository itself is plain Markdown, one file per skill, the same format the ecosystem has already settled on. What is new is the training signal.

Skill curation is a reinforcement-learning problem with an awkward shape. The reward for deciding to write down a particular heuristic today does not arrive until some future task benefits from having read it. The authors handle this by grouping tasks into streams whose dependencies are known in advance: earlier trajectories update the SkillRepo; later, related tasks evaluate those updates and yield the learning signal. A composite reward combines direct task success with how often the curator’s edits are actually consulted downstream.

What the curator learns

SkillOS beats memory-free and strong memory-based baselines on both multi-turn agentic benchmarks and single-turn reasoning tasks, and a curator trained against one executor backbone transfers to a different one with most of the gain intact. The harder-to-dismiss finding is qualitative: over training, the skills produced by the curator evolve from flat procedural notes into richly structured Markdown files — documents that encode higher-level meta-skills rather than isolated recipes. The curator, left to its own devices, rediscovers the organisational moves that the best human skill-authors were already making.

A consequence worth spelling out If the curator produces skill files that look like the ones a careful human would write, and if those files remain Markdown in the canonical format, then a learned curator and a human author can populate the same repository. The boundary between “written by a researcher” and “written by an agent that learned to write” becomes observational rather than structural. That matters both as a promise — ecosystems can self-fill their gaps — and as a caution: provenance tracking of the kind ARA built into its four-layer protocol becomes more, not less, important.

ARA gives a skill its anatomy. SkillOS gives it its origin. A third question sits between the two: once a skill exists, how does the agent that needs it find it amid hundreds of siblings, and load only as much of it as the current decision requires? A smaller paper from Peking University, posted a week before ARA, answers exactly that.

VIIThe practical frontier · how a skill is found

A third 2026 paper turns the practical question of skill management into a representation question — and finds an answer sharp enough to evaluate.

Sections V and VI were about what agents do — reproduce papers, learn to curate their own libraries. There is a more mundane question underneath both, and it is the one that anyone with a ~/.claude/skills/ folder discovers within a week of use: how should a skill library be organised so that the agent finds the right skill at the right moment, and reads only as much of it as it needs? Practitioners call this progressive disclosure, and until recently it was treated as a prompt-engineering problem: write the skill's description in the YAML header tightly enough that the agent can triage on keywords alone, and keep the bulk of the prose in references/ files that load on demand. It works, but only up to the point where the library is small enough for one author to remember.

Perplexity’s May 2026 engineering guide, Designing, Refining, and Maintaining Agent Skills at Perplexity, is useful because it describes the same problem from production rather than from a paper. Their blunt rule is that a skill is context, not ordinary software. The directory layout is the hub-and-spoke interface: SKILL.md for routing and main instructions, scripts/ for deterministic logic the agent should run rather than reinvent, references/ for heavy documents, assets/ for templates and schemas, and config.json for first-run user setup. That mirrors the public Agent Skills specification, but Perplexity adds the operator’s warning: every visible skill taxes every session, so the library itself has to be treated as an engineered runtime surface.

In late April 2026, a group from Peking University (Liang, Wang, Liang & Liu) argued that progressive disclosure is really a representation problem, and proposed a solution sharp enough to evaluate. Their paper — From Skill Text to Skill Structure — points out that a SKILL.md file entangles three very different kinds of information in a single textual surface: scheduling (when should this skill be invoked? what does it take and return?), structural (what phases does its execution fall into, and how do they connect?), and logical (inside each phase, what concrete actions happen, what resources do they touch, what could go wrong?). Keeping them in one Markdown blob is fine when a human is doing the retrieval, but forces a machine to reason over all three axes simultaneously each time it reaches for the file.

The SSL representation

The three layers map onto three different retrieval questions. When an agent is asking which skill should I use?, only the scheduling layer matters — a compact capability record. When it is asking how does this skill unfold?, the structural layer provides the scene graph. When it finally executes, the logical layer supplies concrete action/resource evidence and the associated risks. Each layer is loaded on demand, and each is grounded back to the exact span in the original SKILL.md that produced it — so the source document remains the ground truth and the structured record remains an interface over it, never a replacement.

The gains are not dramatic, but they are consistent and in the right direction. More interestingly, the tasks exercise different layers: Skill Discovery leans on scheduling and structural evidence (the "what does this skill do?" question), Risk Assessment leans on logical evidence (the "what could this skill touch, and how badly?" question). Both improve when the evidence they need is typed and separated from the prose that surrounds it.

Why this is the right framing of progressive disclosure In the practitioner community, progressive disclosure usually means "keep the YAML header short so the agent can triage, and push detail into references/". That is a quantitative cut — less prose up front, more on demand. SSL suggests a qualitative cut instead: at every depth, load only the kind of evidence the current decision needs. Triage reads the scheduling layer. Planning reads the structural layer. Execution and safety checks read the logical layer. The source document remains for anything the structure cannot anchor — rationale, examples, caveats — which is what an authored document is uniquely good at.

Perplexity’s production version of the same idea

Perplexity makes the cost model concrete. The Index tier loads only each visible skill’s name: description, roughly a hundred tokens per skill, for every session and every user. The Load tier brings in the full SKILL.md body, on the order of thousands of tokens, after the agent chooses a skill. The Runtime tier is unbounded but conditional: scripts, references, assets, subskills, formatting files, and special-case documents are paid for only when the agent actually reads or executes them.

This is where Perplexity and SSL fit together cleanly. Perplexity gives the operational budget: index, load, runtime. SSL gives the typed representation that can make those budget cuts less ad hoc: scheduling for the index, structure for the loaded plan, logic for runtime evidence and risk. The lesson is not merely “make skills shorter”. It is to put the right kind of knowledge at the right context depth.

Maintenance implication The highest-value material is usually negative: gotchas, forbidden loads, known failures, and neighbor-confusion examples. Perplexity recommends writing evals before the skill, testing whether the skill loads when it should and stays silent when it should not, and treating post-merge maintenance as an append-mostly gotcha flywheel. That advice also explains the “action at a distance” problem: adding one skill can make a seemingly unrelated skill route worse, because the index is a shared decision surface.

SkillsBench gives this caution empirical teeth. Its arXiv abstract reports that curated skills raised average pass rate by 16.2 percentage points across 86 tasks and 7,308 trajectories, but effects varied widely, 16 of 84 tasks had negative deltas, and self-generated skills provided no average benefit. Focused skills with two or three modules outperformed comprehensive documentation. For this report, that result matters because it keeps SkillOS honest: an autonomous curator may propose skills, but promotion into a shared repository still needs route evals, progressive-loading evals, end-to-end task evals, and cross-model checks.

A stack is starting to appear

Three system papers, within eight weeks of each other, arrived at three different faces of the same object. ARA gives a skill its anatomy; SkillOS gives it its origin; SSL gives it a retrieval layer. None of them was written as a response to the others; none cites the others. And yet they fit together with an ease that suggests the real structure underneath is not theirs — it is the structure of the object they are all trying to manage. The next section makes that structure explicit.

VIIIThe lifecycle of a skill

The three system papers we have just read are not answers to different questions. They are answers to the same question, asked about different stages of one object's life — and a new survey now makes that lifecycle legible.

ARA, SkillOS, and SSL do not share an architecture, an evaluation, or a group of authors. They share a premise. All three assume a world in which skills — reusable units of agent capability — exist as persistent, first-class objects in a library, and all three attack a different operational problem in that world. ARA asks what a skill contains when research produces it; SkillOS asks what policy decides when to write one; SSL asks what representation makes one findable. Read separately they sound like three unrelated proposals. Read with their related-work sections open, they fall into place as three moves on a single board.

External check: the CUHK-Shenzhen agent-skills survey On 8 May 2026, Yingli Zhou, Shu Wang, Yaodong Su, Wenchuan Du, Yixiang Fang, and Xuemin Lin at The Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen posted A Comprehensive Survey on Agent Skills: Taxonomy, Techniques, and Applications to arXiv (2605.07358, cs.IR). It is useful precisely because it is not selling a single algorithm. It reads the fragmented literature on LLM agents, tool use, long-term memory, automated workflows, and self-evolving agents through one object: the agent skill.

The survey sharpens the sentence this report has been circling around: tools answer whether an agent can reach a capability; skills encode how that capability should be used reliably, repeatedly, and under constraints. In the survey’s framing, MCP, function calling, scripts, and APIs solve access and interoperability. They do not decide when search beats memory, how several tools should be sequenced, what to do when an output is malformed, or how completion should be checked. That missing layer is the procedural gap. A skill is the artifact that narrows it.

It is a governed operating layer: routing rules, stage gates, anti-rationalization checks, reference material, and review personas that make the agent slower at the moments where speed would create risk. For autonomous science, this reinforces a conservative direction: skills should not merely help an agent write faster; they should constrain what it is allowed to claim.

Add — when is an experience worth codifying?

The question of when a trajectory becomes a skill has the longest lineage. Voyager (Wang et al., 2023) gave the operational answer: if a piece of code passes self-verification and completes a curriculum-generated goal, commit it. That works in Minecraft because success is machine-checkable. In looser domains the criterion fails, and the literature splits in two.

One branch treats skills as abstracted query-response pairs — distil successful trajectories into reusable insights and store them for retrieval. This is the line from Reflexion (ephemeral self-notes) through ExpeL (persistent cross-task insights) to Synapse and ReasoningBank. The other branch extracts recurring patterns across many trajectories, not from individual successes — Agent Workflow Memory (Wang et al., 2024) is its clearest statement: a routine that appears once is a solution; a routine that appears three times is a workflow. Buffer-of-Thoughts (Yang et al., 2024) does the same at the template level.

SkillOS sits at the endpoint of this trajectory. Rather than writing a rule for when to add, it trains a curator to make the decision, and is rewarded when its edits help downstream tasks. Across the full arc — Voyager to ExpeL to AWM to SkillOS — the centre of gravity of the add verb has moved from “does this succeed?” to “does this generalise?” to “does an RL-trained curator think this is worth codifying?”.

Query — finding the right skill, and loading only what matters

Once a library is large, retrieval splits into two questions. Which skill to pick, and how much of it to load. The skill-retrieval literature — Sentence-BERT, DPR, BEIR as the generic substrate; CRAFT, ToolRet, ToolReRank, MassTool as the tool-specific refinement — has accumulated evidence that naive vector search over skill descriptions does not scale: capability matching depends on signals scattered across interface fields, examples, dependencies, and structural detail. SSL’s contribution to this thread is not another retriever but a representation: expose the signals as a typed record so that each retrieval decision can consult only the layer it needs.

The “how much to load” question has a parallel thread in the memory community. MemGPT (Packer et al., 2023) proposed virtual context management modelled on operating-system paging; Mem0 (Chhikara et al., 2025) industrialised the idea into a production store with dynamic extraction and consolidation; A-MEM (Xu et al., 2025) lets the LLM itself edit the memory graph, linking related entries and collapsing duplicates. Representation (SSL) and substrate (MemGPT / Mem0 / A-MEM) are currently tracking parallel; the interesting question is where they will meet.

Modify — editing a skill from experience

A skill that never changes after it is written is not a skill but a snippet. Modification has, until recently, been an ephemeral operation. Reflexion’s post-mortems evaporate after the task. Voyager’s iterative prompting edits only the code inside the single skill. ExpeL persists insights but does not rewrite the skill entries they qualify. The pattern is consistent: modification is the verb everyone supports in principle and very few systems implement as a first-class library operation.

Two lines are changing that. The heuristic line — A-MEM and Alita — lets an LLM execute fixed memory operations (link, merge, annotate) on the library between turns, using prompted rules rather than a trained policy. The learned line — SkillOS, and a handful of concurrent works SkillOS groups under “learned memory operation” — trains the curator to decide which operation to apply from delayed task reward. Both lines agree on the premise: modification is not a human review step, it is a feedback channel from the environment into the artifact. They disagree on whether the channel is wired by prompt or by gradient.

Delete — the verb nobody wants to talk about

Four verbs in, and the literature is pleasantly crowded. The fifth is where the ground gets thin. Almost every system we have discussed is designed to grow its skill library; almost none is designed to shrink it. The name for this when it is spoken at all is consolidation; the more honest name is forgetting.

Mem0 implements dynamic consolidation explicitly — new memory extracts and merges existing entries rather than appending to them. A-MEM goes further, letting the LLM collapse duplicates as the library matures. Neither is yet a skill-library system in the strict sense, but they sketch the machinery that is missing. ARA’s own Future Work calls this out as the most pressing near-term gap: “like code repositories, Aras decay without maintenance as dependencies rot and practices evolve, yet unmaintained artifacts are the community norm.”

Why this matters more than it sounds A skill library that only grows is a library that, within a year, becomes unusable. Duplicate skills compete for the same retrieval slot; outdated skills mislead planning; stale heuristics poison the training signal of any curator learning on top. Every mature software system accepts that most of its code will eventually be deleted. Skill libraries are software; the same arithmetic applies. The question is not whether to forget, but how to forget safely — which edits are merges, which are deprecations, and which are the kind of delete that a later context may regret.

Evolve — the fifth verb, and the missing literature

The fifth verb does not fit CRUD, and it is the one the current literature is furthest from solving. Evolve is the observation that in the presence of real users the same nominal skill — “write a paper introduction”, say, or “plan a DID analysis” — diverges by task and by person. Researcher A writes introductions against a Keith Head rubric; researcher B writes them against a Samuel Arbesman rubric; both are fluent speakers of the same skill, and both are right. A skill that insists on being a single canonical file erases that difference. A skill library built for experts must preserve it.

The building blocks exist; no current system assembles them. ARA gives every entry a provenance tag distinguishing what a human contributed from what an agent proposed, and its Future Work section proposes treating each artifact’s contribution as a structured diff against its parents — the closest the literature comes to naming the evolve verb. SkillOS shows that the curator role can be learned rather than authored. SSL shows that the layers of a skill can be reasoned about independently. Put the three together and a concrete mechanism appears: a skill becomes a base record plus a set of per-user patches, each with its own provenance, its own structural layer, and its own reward history under the curator. Different experts do not fork the base; they inherit from it, and what they change is visible to everyone who queries with their identity.

None of the papers we have read implements this yet. It is the work the next year is most likely to produce, and the shape it should take is already visible from where the three 2026 papers meet.

IXFour thinking postures for autonomous scientific work

Sections V–VIII gave the agent its instruments. This one gives it a discipline.

Every piece of infrastructure assembled above — the ARA artifact, the SkillOS curator, the SSL retrieval layer, the lifecycle verbs — eventually resolves to one moment: an agent has the right skill loaded, and now has to think. What happens inside that moment is not fully covered by any single paper above. The most common failure mode in real scientific-agent systems is not a missing skill but a mixed reasoning step — asking for assumptions and also committing to an answer, appealing to first principles and also deferring to precedent, staging a contradiction and also flattening it. The four postures below are a mnemonic for keeping those moves separate.

Core story

Ask like Socrates. Decompose like Musk. See conflict like Marx. Close the loop like Qian.

The four figures below are not a claim of intellectual priority. They are a mnemonic for four mutually exclusive operating postures an agent can adopt during scientific work. The point is not to decorate the system with famous names; it is to prevent the most common failure mode in agent reasoning: mixing incompatible moves inside one step.

The unifying logic is bias reduction. Each posture attacks one class of error that appears repeatedly in scientific-agent systems. Socratic inquiry reduces premise bias: assumptions the team did not know it had. First-principles decomposition reduces analogy bias: copying the field's current recipe instead of reasoning from the lowest constraints available. The hard gate matters. In the Socratic posture, the agent may ask and classify but must not answer. In the first-principles posture, it may derive but must not borrow authority from what other labs do. In the dialectical posture, it may map contradictions but must not flatten them into a bland trade-off. In the cybernetic posture, it may operationalize but must not sell a vision without observability. These constraints are the shackles; scientific imagination is the dance allowed inside them.

XIZero-hallucination evidence

Citation verification is the smallest useful version of a larger problem: a research agent should never promote a claim, result, or branch in the discovery process without a checkable receipt.

The community evidence is unusually practical here. The GitHub ecosystem has moved beyond generic “write better papers” prompts toward executable academic workflows: AI-Research-SKILLs packages ML paper writing, LaTeX, and citation APIs; academic-research-skills wraps research, writing, review, revision, and finalization; claude-scholar adds Zotero-centered reference management; GPT Academic shows how much demand exists for interactive paper reading, polishing, translation, and code explanation; and the empirical-research collection indexes 119 repositories and 23,000+ skills across social-science workflows. The exact star counts move daily, so the meaningful signal is not the number. It is that citation hygiene, evidence propagation, and reference management are becoming infrastructure.

The empirical pressure is real. In the OpenScholar study in Nature, the authors introduce a retrieval-augmented system and ScholarQABench for long-form scientific synthesis. Their most relevant finding for this report is not that retrieval helps; it is that citation accuracy becomes measurable. The paper reports a 45-million-paper open-access store, a 2,967-query benchmark, and GPT-4o citation hallucination rates of 78–90% in its setting, while OpenScholar's retrieval store, retrievers, and self-feedback loop brought citation-backed synthesis close to expert-level citation accuracy. That is the right lesson: no-hallucination writing is not a tone style. It is a retrieval and verification architecture.

The first hard rule A model may suggest a reference, but it may not write a reference from memory. A citation enters the paper only after an external source of record confirms the work exists, its metadata matches, and the specific claim being cited is actually supported by that work. If verification fails, the system must drop the citation or leave an explicit [CITATION NEEDED] marker.

What the current skills already know

Three local sources converge on the same workflow. The vendored AI-Research-SKILLs citation guide defines a five-step path: search, verify in multiple sources, retrieve BibTeX through DOI or a trusted metadata service, validate the claim, and only then add the entry. The claude-scholar citation skill adds a layered validator: BibTeX format, existence, metadata matching, and content validation. RebuttalStudio adapts the same rule for the highest-pressure setting: never introduce a reviewer-facing citation unless it has been checked.

Team memory: shared state without shared hallucination

Multi-agent memory is now visible enough to be treated as a separate design problem. The broad surveys agree on the vocabulary shift: Memory in the Age of AI Agents separates memory from ordinary RAG and context engineering, while Memory for Autonomous LLM Agents formalizes memory as a write–manage–read loop with engineering problems such as write filtering, contradiction handling, latency, and privacy governance. Once there is more than one agent, those problems become distributed-systems problems: who may write, who may read, whose view is authoritative, and which memory survives into the project record.

The strongest recent answer is not “put every agent in the same vector store”. Collaborative Memory explicitly separates private fragments from selectively shared fragments, uses a dynamic access graph over users, agents, and resources, and keeps immutable provenance so later policy checks can ask who created a memory, which resources it touched, and when. Mesh Memory Protocol reaches a compatible conclusion from the agent-to-agent side: each peer has its own identity and memory, accepts peer content field-by-field rather than wholesale, preserves lineage through content hashes, and stores the receiver’s role-evaluated understanding rather than blindly copying raw peer signals.

Programmable memory runtime: why Rust is interesting

A newer implementation pattern moves memory out of Markdown and into a small local runtime. The point is easy to misunderstand: writing memory in Rust does not by itself reduce token use. Tokens are saved only when the runtime filters, ranks, summarizes, enforces access policy, and returns a compact memory object instead of dumping whole transcripts into context. Rust is interesting because it is a strong fit for that runtime layer: fast local search, low background overhead, strong typing, safe concurrency, durable append-only logs, and hard permission boundaries.

This implementation pattern is part of a broader move toward treating memory as code rather than as notes. The nearest public examples include codemem, which presents itself as a standalone Rust memory engine for coding agents, Smriti, a Rust-based experimental memory engine that combines graph ranking and vector-style recall, and systems such as Sediment, which stores agent memory as structured SQLite-backed context layers rather than loose prose. These are not proof that Rust is the only correct language; they are evidence that memory is becoming an executable subsystem.

The important lesson is not that software engineering equals science. It is that both domains need the same control discipline once AI agents start doing real work: role separation, stage gates, explicit receipts, and a human owner who decides what gets promoted. These engineering systems are useful analogs for autonomous scientific discovery because they preserve speed while forcing the agent to pass through explicit checks. The final scientific object is not a deployable app but a claim graph that must stay faithful to the evidence.

Verification counterpart: LLM-as-a-Verifier

A second useful counterpart is LLM-as-a-Verifier, a Stanford, Berkeley, and NVIDIA project about long-horizon agent evaluation. Strip away the social-media headline and the technical claim is simple: many coding agents can solve a task if they are sampled many times, but the harness often cannot identify which trajectory is the successful one. The bottleneck is no longer only generation. It is selection.

The framework replaces coarse LLM-as-a-Judge scoring with pairwise verification. Instead of asking for one discrete score, it reads top-log-probabilities over many score tokens, repeats the comparison, and decomposes evaluation into criteria. In Terminal-Bench-style coding tasks those criteria include specification adherence, output format, and visible error signals; for SWE-Bench-style tasks they include root cause, code quality, and empirical verification. Candidate trajectories then enter a round-robin tournament, and the path with the strongest pairwise record is selected.

Verifier moveWhy it matters for agentsAutonomous-science analogue
Fine-grained score tokens Reduces ties that appear when long trajectories receive the same coarse 1–8 score. Rank competing hypotheses or experiment plans by support strength, not by a vague overall impression.
Repeated verification Turns a noisy single judgment into a more stable estimate. Require repeated audit passes before a claim moves from tentative to supported.
Criteria decomposition Separates spec compliance, output match, and error evidence instead of hiding them in one score. Score citation validity, experiment fidelity, statistical support, and claim scope separately.
Round-robin selection Selects the best trajectory from many candidate agent runs. Choose which branch in the evidence web should be promoted, repeated, rejected, or escalated to a human.

The boundary matters. A verifier score is not a receipt. In software, the selected trajectory still has to pass tests. In science, the selected branch still has to bind to citations, logs, raw tables, code, and replication artifacts. The right integration is therefore not “let the verifier decide truth”. It is: use verification compute to triage many possible paths, then promote only the paths whose hard evidence survives the ARA-style ledger.

XIIWeb search as a tool — what an agent calls when memory ends

When agent memory ends and the open web begins, the choice of MCP server stops being cosmetic. Three layers, three generations, thirteen tools, and three picks for an academic workflow that is allergic to new subscriptions.

Section VII asked how an agent finds the right skill in its own library. This section asks the older question — what the agent does when none of its skills hold the answer. Skill retrieval is the inward problem: which procedure to run. Web retrieval is the outward one: what does the open web know, and how do I make it citable. Two years ago the answer was a line of code that called Bing. As of mid-2026 it is a sub-ecosystem of MCP servers, hosted research agents, and academic-source connectors, and the choice between them matters because they return materially different things — sometimes a list of links, sometimes clean Markdown, sometimes a finished report with footnotes — at price points that span four orders of magnitude.

Three problems hidden in one verb

“Search the web” sounds atomic, but every working agent system splits it into three jobs that fail in different ways and want different infrastructure.

Query. Translate a natural-language intent into a ranked list of candidate URLs and snippets. This is the historical search-engine problem; what changes when an LLM is the customer is that snippet quality and citation completeness matter more than ranking depth, because the agent will only inspect the top few results before deciding whether to fetch. Tavily, Brave, Bing, Kagi, and Exa are query-layer providers in this sense, even when their pitch deck says otherwise.

Fetch. Pull a target page’s body, render dynamic content if needed, strip ads and chrome, and produce something the model can read. The output format is decisive. Raw HTML is unusable; cleaned Markdown is the de facto standard; structured extractions (title + authors + publication date + main body) are what citation-grade workflows actually need. A search backend that returns ten URLs and a fetch backend that turns each URL into clean Markdown are not the same product, and conflating them is the most common mistake when picking a tool. Firecrawl, Jina’s Reader (r.jina.ai), and Tavily-extract live here.

Synthesize. Take many fetches and produce a multi-source answer with attributable claims. This is the most expensive step and the one that LLM vendors have begun to commoditise: Perplexity’s sonar-deep-research, Exa’s /research, Linkup’s research endpoint, OpenAI’s Deep Research mode, Anthropic’s Research connectors. They are not search engines; they are agents-as-a-service that bill per task and return a write-up with footnotes. Whether to use one depends on whether the agent doing the calling trusts the synthesizer’s reasoning more than its own.

The taxonomy is not pedantry. A research workflow needs at least one provider for each layer. Tavily can do all three but does the synthesize layer poorly; Firecrawl does layer two well and layer one as an afterthought; arxiv-mcp-server does layer one but only over arXiv. Treating them as substitutes is the source of most “this MCP is not very good” complaints — usually the agent was sent to a query-layer tool when what it needed was synthesis, or vice versa.

Three generations, and what each one fixed

The current zoo reads more legibly chronologically. Three generations of tooling have appeared in the last twenty-four months, and each one solves a problem the previous one made visible.

First generation: search APIs in MCP clothing. Bing, Brave, Tavily, Kagi, and Exa already had developer APIs. Wrapping them in an MCP server was the obvious first move once Anthropic published the Model Context Protocol in late 2024. The 2.0k-star Tavily MCP and 1.0k-star Brave Search MCP are the canonical exemplars: they accept a query and return JSON with title/url/snippet, often with a synthesised answer string for clients that do not want to read snippets. They are cheap, fast, and unopinionated. They also leave the hard parts unsolved — the agent still has to fetch, clean, and decide which result to trust.

Second generation: fetch and crawl as first-class tools. Firecrawl (6.3k ☆, the highest-starred web-tool MCP on GitHub) and Jina AI’s Reader (r.jina.ai, exposed as part of the 685-star jina-ai/MCP server) recognised that an agent reading the web needs Markdown more than it needs URLs. They built fetchers that handle JavaScript rendering, ad removal, PDF extraction, and the occasional anti-bot challenge, and they exposed those as MCP tools alongside a thin search layer. This is the generation that made it feasible for agents to read the web at all, rather than guessing from snippets.

Third generation: research agents on the other end of an API. Perplexity’s sonar-deep-research model, Exa’s /research endpoint, and Linkup’s research API took the full query-fetch-synthesize loop, ran it on their infrastructure, and exposed the result as a single tool call. The agent says “research X for fifteen minutes”; the service comes back with a report and citations. This generation is the most expensive (Linkup’s deep-research tier runs $0.25 to $2.50 per request as of May 2026; Exa Deep Reasoning is $15 per thousand requests) and the most opinionated. It is the right tool when the question is bounded and the agent has no good reason to interleave its own reasoning with the search. It is the wrong tool when the search is one move in a longer chain.

AnySearch, the newcomer that prompted this section, is not a fourth generation. It is an attempt to package generations one and two behind a single brand — a unified connector with general search, twenty-three vertical “domains” (finance, academic, legal, code, security, IP, energy, geo, and more), parallel batch search, and URL-to-Markdown extraction in one MCP. Whether that bundling is a real differentiator is a question we return to two subsections down.

AnySearch — what “unify” actually means

AnySearch is younger than thirty days at time of writing. The repository (anysearch-ai/anysearch-mcp-server) was created on 2026-04-30 and last pushed on 2026-05-07, with 311 stars, 33 forks, no LICENSE file, and no /about page on the company website. The README is technically credible: it documents Streamable HTTP transport (MCP spec 2025-03-26), an anonymous tier alongside bearer-token authentication, four tools (search / list_domains / batch_search / extract), a discipline of calling list_domains before any vertical search to discover the per-domain query format and parameter schema, and twenty-two named verticals (the project description says twenty-three — a small inconsistency that has not yet been reconciled in the README). Its claimed differentiator versus mcp-omnisearch is real: omnisearch is a fan-out router that requires the user to bring API keys for Tavily, Brave, Kagi, Exa, Linkup, and Firecrawl; AnySearch presents itself as a single backend that owns its own indexes. Whether that backend is a proprietary crawl or a re-export of someone else’s data is not disclosed in the README, and the pricing page (anysearch.com/pricing) returns 404 today. The LICENSE=NONE matters more than it sounds: the server code is not legally redistributable, so a research collection that tries to vendor it for reproducibility cannot. The right read for a research artefact that may have to compile years from now: technically plausible, momentum-watch, do not vendor yet.

Three picks for Claude Code in an academic workflow

The selection constraints are explicit: (i) no new paid SaaS subscription required — a free tier or pay-per-call API key against an account the project would have anyway is acceptable, (ii) returns structured citations the agent can hand to RebuttalStudio’s verifier, (iii) plugs into the existing skill stack at /Users/mjm/Skill — deep-research, academic-paper, rigor-reviewer, and the citation skills surveyed in § XI. Three tools meet all three at once and, taken together, cover layers one and two of the taxonomy without a layer-three dependency.

1. paper-search-mcp (1.5k ☆, MIT, free). The clearest fit. It is a single MCP that searches arXiv, PubMed, bioRxiv, medRxiv, Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex, Crossref, CORE, Europe PMC, PubMed Central, dblp, OpenAIRE, CiteSeerX, DOAJ, BASE, Zenodo, HAL, SSRN, and IACR ePrint, with Unpaywall fallback for DOI resolution and an open-access-first download chain. Output is a standardised Paper class carrying DOI, authors, abstract, and PDF link. This is exactly what rigor-reviewer was built to verify against — a structured citation, not a snippet. Trade-off versus Jina’s search_arxiv / search_ssrn: Jina covers two indices and is metered; paper-search-mcp covers nineteen and is free. Trade-off versus blazickjp/arxiv-mcp-server (2.7k ☆): the arXiv-only server is more polished as a single-source tool but does not federate. Use paper-search-mcp as the default for the deep-research skill’s literature-canvas step.

Install caveat — this matters in practice. The PyPI release is v0.1.3 from 2025-04-29 and has not been re-cut in over thirteen months, while main has shipped fixes for arXiv relevance-sort (#73; the PyPI search_arxiv still hardcodes sortBy='submittedDate', so canonical papers like ReAct or CoT never surface in the top-10), Semantic Scholar NoneType crashes (#68), Zenodo isoformat crashes (#69), and the missing uvx entry-point (#64). The right install path is therefore not uvx paper-search-mcp or pip install paper-search-mcp; it is what the project’s own README lists first — clone the repo and load it as a Claude Code skill that runs from source via uv run --directory <REPO_PATH> paper-search .... Two further real-world ceilings are worth knowing before relying on a comprehensive run: Google Scholar reliably rate-limits after about ten queries per session unless a proxy is set (#74), and only seven of the nineteen sources are full-text-reliable in the project’s own capability matrix (arXiv, bioRxiv, medRxiv, IACR, Zenodo, HAL, plus Semantic Scholar and PMC for OA-only); the rest are either metadata-only (PubMed, Crossref, OpenAlex, dblp, OpenAIRE) or unstable (SSRN, CiteSeerX, BASE) or skeleton-only (IEEE, ACM). None of this disqualifies the recommendation; it changes the install command and means a serious literature canvas should treat paper-search-mcp as arXiv-plus-bioRxiv-plus-Semantic-Scholar with metadata-enrichment, not as a no-asterisks unified academic search.

2. Tavily MCP (2.0k ☆, MIT server, generous free tier). The general-web complement. paper-search-mcp covers the academic indices but cannot read a lab website, a workshop call-for-papers, or a preprint announcement that is only on Twitter. Tavily can — at 1,000 credits per month with no credit card required, with first-class Claude Code installation (claude mcp add --transport http tavily https://mcp.tavily.com/mcp), and four tools (search / extract / map / crawl) that cover layers one and two. Trade-off versus Exa: Exa’s neural ranking is genuinely better on English-language semantic queries and its findSimilar is unique, but Exa’s free MCP tier was capped in February 2026 and its $7 / 1k pricing is harder to justify on day-zero exploration. Trade-off versus Anthropic’s built-in web_search: that tool is already wired into Claude Code (no MCP to install) but charges $10 / 1k on the API and returns less structured output than Tavily’s extract.

3. Jina AI MCP (685 ☆, Apache-2.0). The fetcher and the academic enricher. Three tools matter for our stack: read_url and parallel_read_url (URL-to-Markdown via r.jina.ai, often free even without an API key), extract_pdf (figures, tables, and equations from arXiv PDFs or any PDF URL — directly relevant to figures4papers and nature-figure), and search_bibtex (academic papers returned as BibTeX from DBLP plus Semantic Scholar — directly relevant to the citation skills discussed in § XI). It is the only candidate in the table that has all of arXiv search, SSRN search, BibTeX, PDF extraction, and a reranker in one MCP. The composition is intentional. paper-search-mcp does the academic query layer; Tavily does the general-web query and basic extract; Jina does the citation-bearing fetch and the PDF/BibTeX work that the existing skill stack already references. None of the three forces a paid subscription on the user. Each one returns citations in a structure rigor-reviewer can verify. Together they supply layer one (paper-search + Tavily search) and layer two (Tavily-extract + Jina-read + Jina-extract-pdf) without a layer-three dependency, which is the right default for an academic agent that wants to keep synthesis local rather than outsourcing it to a hosted research model.

What to avoid (and why)

Three categories of tool look attractive in marketing copy and fail one of the three constraints when actually wired in.

AnySearch, today. The technical design is plausible but LICENSE=NONE makes the server code legally unredistributable, the company has no public /about page, and the pricing page returns 404. For a research artefact that may have to compile years from now, that is too thin a foundation. Watch the project; do not vendor it yet.

Hosted deep-research APIs as the default fetcher. Linkup’s research tier ($0.25–2.50 / request), Exa Deep Reasoning ($15 / 1k), and Perplexity’s sonar-deep-research are the right tools when a single isolated question genuinely benefits from minutes of remote reasoning. They are the wrong tools when the search is one move inside a longer agent loop, because each call bills at enterprise prices and returns a paragraph that is not actually composable with the local agent’s state.

Privacy-first general search as the citation backbone. Brave Search and Kagi are excellent products for human users but mediocre as a citation source: Brave’s free tier caps at 2,000 queries / month and returns no extract, and Kagi’s API requires a paid subscription whose value is in re-ranking rather than coverage. Use them as fallbacks when paper-search-mcp returns nothing, not as the primary.

Anthropic’s built-in web_search as the only tool. It is wired into Claude Code with zero configuration, which is its strength. It is also a generalist search at $10 / 1k whose output is less structured than Tavily’s extract and less academic-aware than paper-search-mcp. It is the right backstop, not the right primary.

The point is not that any of these tools is bad. It is that the three problems hidden inside “search the web” want different products, and the cost of conflating them shows up only after the agent has built a workflow on the wrong layer. Pick by layer first; pick by brand second.

Web search is not one tool with three brands. It is three tools with one brand each — and the cheapest way to get a citation-grade research workflow today is to pick a free academic-source MCP for query, a free general-web MCP for fetch, and stop before the invoice for synthesis arrives.

XIIIThe long-horizon problem

When a research task takes days, not minutes, every architectural assumption about agents breaks. Three axes — task horizon, context scale, and decision frequency — define the space; benchmarks now exist for each.

Most agent benchmarks give a system minutes or hours. A real research project gives it weeks. The gap between these two regimes is not merely quantitative — it is architectural. An agent that can write a function in three minutes may be entirely unable to coordinate a month-long experiment campaign, because the failure modes of long-horizon work are categorically different from those of short tasks. This section maps those failure modes, the benchmarks that measure them, and the emerging solutions — organised along three axes that together define the difficulty surface for autonomous science.

Three axes of difficulty

The literature conflates three distinct scaling challenges under the umbrella of “hard agent tasks”. Separating them clarifies both what breaks and what to measure:

Long horizon — the task spans hours, days, or weeks. The agent must plan, execute, recover from errors, revise plans, and maintain coherence across many context windows. Failure modes: goal forgetting, error cascading, stale plans, coordination overhead.

Large context — the input or accumulated state exceeds what the model can attend to uniformly. The agent must retrieve, prioritise, and forget selectively. Failure modes: lost-in-the-middle bias, context drift, detail loss on compaction.

High frequency — the agent must make many decisions per unit time, iterating rapidly through edit-test-fix cycles or parallel exploration branches. Failure modes: diminishing returns on additional compute, retry storms, cascading regressions.

A Carriage for autonomous science must handle all three simultaneously: a month-long project (long horizon) accumulates millions of tokens of state (large context) while each role makes dozens of tool calls per session (high frequency). The benchmarks below measure each axis; the architectural patterns that follow address their intersection.

What breaks at long horizons

Five failure modes dominate when agent tasks stretch beyond a single context window:

Context drift. The agent’s working memory fills, compacts, and loses detail. Decisions made in hour one become invisible by hour four. The agent re-derives them, sometimes differently, creating internal contradictions that compound silently.

Goal forgetting. A subtask absorbs all attention. The agent optimises locally (make this test pass) while losing the global objective (ship the feature that the test was supposed to verify). In multi-agent systems this is worse: each agent remembers its own subgoal but nobody holds the top-level acceptance criterion.

Error cascading. A wrong assumption in step 3 of 40 propagates silently until step 38, where the system produces a confident but wrong result. Backtracking cost grows superlinearly with horizon length because intermediate state was never checkpointed.

Coordination overhead. In multi-agent setups, the cost of keeping N agents aligned grows faster than the work they produce. Messages, handoffs, shared-state locks, and conflict resolution consume an increasing fraction of total compute. The system becomes a bureaucracy.

Stale plans. A plan written at the start becomes wrong as the environment changes (new data arrives, a dependency breaks, a collaborator publishes first). Agents that cannot revise their own plans mid-execution either follow dead plans or abandon planning entirely and become reactive.

Planning architectures for long tasks

The field has converged on a small number of patterns for sustaining coherent behaviour across long horizons:

Hierarchical task decomposition

The dominant pattern is plan → decompose → dispatch → verify. A coordinator holds the global plan and decomposes it into subtasks small enough for a single context window. AutoGen (Microsoft, 2023), LangGraph, and CrewAI all implement variants.

Plan persistence and revision

A plan that lives only in the context window dies on compaction. The fix is to write the plan to disk and treat it as a living document. Google’s Agent Development Kit (ADK, 2025) takes this approach with session-state persistence.

Exploration DAGs over linear logs

An exploration DAG records hypotheses, experiments, results, and dead ends as nodes with typed edges. The ARA protocol’s trace/exploration_tree.yaml is a static cousin designed for publication rather than runtime navigation.

The METR scaling law: task horizon doubles every 7 months

The single most important empirical finding for autonomous science comes from METR’s longitudinal measurement (arXiv:2503.14499, updated January 2026). They define the 50%-task-completion time horizon: the length of tasks (measured in human-equivalent time) that agents can complete with 50% reliability. This metric has been doubling approximately every 7 months for six years (2019–2025), possibly accelerating to every 4 months in 2024. As of early 2026, METR reports they can “barely measure” Claude Mythos Preview, estimating its 50% horizon at several hours of human work.

If this trend continues, agents will reach multi-day task horizons by late 2026 and multi-week horizons by mid-2027. That is precisely the regime where a Carriage — shared memory, coordination protocol, role boundaries, governance — becomes load-bearing rather than optional.

Production findings: what actually happens at scale

Three production data points ground the benchmark story in reality:

Devin (Cognition): February 2025 update reports ~7.8 minutes average on junior developer tasks. In production code review, catches an average of 2 bugs per PR, with 58% being severe. One large organisation saved 5–10% of total developer time on vulnerability remediation.

DX Research (400+ companies, Nov 2024–Feb 2026): Despite a 65% increase in AI tool usage, median PR throughput rose only 7.76%. The gap between benchmark performance and production impact remains large.

The Retry Storm: An independent analysis found a negative correlation between AI agent contribution frequency and CI/CD workflow success rate. Without proper coordination infrastructure (a Carriage), scaling agent output can actively degrade system health.

An open question: when does more time help?

The most provocative finding from recent scaling work is that giving agents more compute time does not always help. On RE-Bench, doubling the time budget from 2 to 8 hours improved human scores substantially but agent scores only marginally. This suggests that the next frontier is not longer context windows or faster inference, but better intra-task learning: the ability to update beliefs, revise plans, and accumulate evidence within a single extended session. The METR scaling law gives this question a deadline. If the 50%-task-completion horizon continues doubling every 7 months, agents will reach the multi-week regime by mid-2027. At that point, the Carriage is no longer a nice-to-have — it is the difference between capable horses pulling in different directions and a team that can deliver a payload across a continent.

XIII-AThe Carriage thesis

If a harness is what turns an LLM into an agent, then a carriage is what turns a set of agents into a multi-agent system. This section gives the term a load-bearing definition, grounded in the same first principles that gave “harness” its meaning, and read against the architecture of MinionsOS.

The word carriage has been used loosely throughout the previous sections, standing in for “whatever it is that makes N agents a team rather than a crowd”. That informality is no longer tenable. With the long-horizon problem framed in Section XIII and the portable-memory problem framed in Section XIII-B, the Carriage sits exactly between them — and a working definition is the prerequisite for any further design discussion. The argument below proceeds in four moves: it retraces how harness was reverse-engineered by the agent-evaluation community, then applies the same construction to carriage, then derives a sharper definition from the structural cause of multi-agent failure (correlated hallucination), and finally reads MinionsOS as one instance of that definition.

How “harness” was earned

“Harness” was not coined by a paper. It was reverse-engineered by the agent-evaluation community when a stubborn empirical fact refused to go away: the same model, evaluated under different scaffolding, produced wildly different capability scores. METR’s longitudinal measurement (Section XIII, the seven-month doubling law) only makes sense if the model and the scaffolding are measured separately. The earliest proxy term was scaffolding — ReAct, AutoGPT, LangChain — but scaffolding implies temporary support. By 2024 the field had standardised on harness, with three load-bearing properties:

  1. Irreducibility. Without a harness, an LLM cannot transform raw capability into agent behaviour. Tool use, memory, reflection — none of these happen inside the weights.
  2. Structural separability. The harness is external to the model and can be swapped without retraining. METR demonstrates this by holding the harness fixed while the model varies.
  3. Capability amplification. Same model + different harness $\Rightarrow$ different effective capability ceiling. The harness is a multiplier, not an additive component.

The horse-and-harness metaphor is precise: the horse’s power is unchanged, but without the harness it cannot pull a load. The harness directs and constrains force until force becomes useful work. This precision is what we want to preserve when we move to the next abstraction layer.

Building the symmetric definition for “carriage”

A definition of carriage that lives at the same abstraction layer as harness must satisfy three symmetric properties:

  1. Irreducibility. Without a carriage, $\sum$ Agent cannot transform into multi-agent behaviour. Coordination, shared state, and collective decisions do not happen spontaneously inside individual agents.
  2. Structural separability. The carriage is external to any single agent and can be swapped without rewriting agents. Replace EACN with another event bus, replace the Library with another knowledge layer — the agents themselves are unchanged.
  3. Collective amplification. Same agents + different carriage $\Rightarrow$ different effective collective capability. A poor carriage produces a bureaucracy whose work output decreases with team size.

This yields a working definition:

Working definition A carriage is the external structure that turns $\sum$ Agent from a set of independent individuals into a multi-agent system whose error distribution is decorrelated. It is to multi-agent systems what a harness is to single agents: irreducible, structurally separable, and a multiplier of effective capability.

Three filters — what counts, and what does not

The definition is only useful if it can rule things out. Three filters apply to any proposed component:

  1. Solo-usability filter. Can a single agent use this component on its own? If yes, it belongs to the harness, not the carriage. Tool calls, retrieval, and self-reflection all live inside an individual agent’s harness.
  2. Removal filter. If this component is removed, does $\sum$ Agent regress to a set of isolated individuals? If yes, it is part of the carriage. The shared state layer fails this test in the cleanest way: take it away, and agents stop being able to depend on each other’s work.
  3. Decoupling filter. Does the component require modifications inside individual agents to function? If yes, it is an extension of the harness, not the carriage. A carriage component must be agent-agnostic.

The deeper why: correlated hallucination is the failure mode

The three filters tell us which components are part of the carriage. They do not tell us why a carriage is needed at all. The structural answer comes from a property of the agents themselves: an LLM cannot reliably detect its own hallucinations. This is now well-established:

  • Huang et al., ICLR 2024 — Large Language Models Cannot Self-Correct Reasoning Yet. Without an external reward signal, intrinsic self-correction reliably degrades performance on reasoning tasks.
  • Tyen et al., ACL 2024 — LLMs cannot find reasoning errors, but can correct them. Generators and verifiers share the same parametric biases, so error detection requires a signal from outside the model; once an error is pointed out, correction works.
  • Kadavath et al., 2022 — Language Models (Mostly) Know What They Know. Shallow calibration holds at the token level but deteriorates sharply on multi-step reasoning — precisely where multi-agent work lives.
  • Stechly, Valmeekam, Kambhampati, 2024 — On the Self-Verification Limitations of LLMs on Reasoning and Planning Tasks. A formal demonstration that self-verification fails on planning problems.

The shared lesson: an error generator and an error detector that draw from the same parametric distribution will fail together. Self-correction is bounded by the same biases that produced the error.

Now consider what happens when we run $N$ such agents in parallel. If their error distributions were independent, the probability of all $N$ agents missing the same mistake would fall as $\varepsilon^N$ — an exponential in team size. This is the entire promise of multi-agent systems. But the premise is brittle:

The decorrelation principle Multi-agent systems gain reliability only to the extent that their constituent agents make independent errors. When $N$ agents share the same model, the same prompt, and the same context, the effective $N$ collapses to 1. The carriage’s primary job is not coordination — it is engineering decorrelation.

From this angle, design choices that previously looked like cosmetic separation of concerns become load-bearing:

  • Distinct roles with distinct prompts — Coder, Writer, Ethics, Expert — produce conditioning that drifts the error distribution apart across agents.
  • Heterogeneous backbones — Claude alongside Codex GPT-5.5 — further separates failure modes by mixing training data and RLHF lineages.
  • Raw-trajectory storage — Section XIII-B’s argument against consolidation now reads as a decorrelation argument: a summary written by one agent inherits that agent’s biases; downstream agents reading the summary inherit them too. Storing the raw trajectory keeps each downstream agent’s error distribution independent of the upstream agent’s.
  • Independent audit channels — Ethics reads the artefact, not the producing agent’s prompt, so its judgements are not contaminated by the producer’s framing.

The minimal carriage and what hangs off it

With the decorrelation principle in hand, the carriage’s structure can be stated minimally:

Carriage = Protocol  ⊕  Shared State
subject to: error decorrelation across $\sum$ Agent

Protocol is what lets agents see one another’s outputs and align beliefs at decision points. Without it, agents act on inconsistent premises — decision fragmentation, a failure mode strictly worse than a single agent acting alone.

Shared state is what they see in — specifically, raw trajectory rather than consolidated summary, so a hallucination in one agent does not become a fact for the next.

The decorrelation requirement is not a third structural component. It is a property the carriage must satisfy, in the same way a harness must satisfy tool grounding. Hallucination-reduction mechanisms — Noter as record-keeper, Ethics as auditor, Signboard as consensus gate — are implementations of the property, not separate pillars. Each addresses a distinct multi-agent failure mode that single-agent systems do not exhibit:

The capability formula

Putting the structure and the property together yields a more honest formula for multi-agent capability than naïve summation:

Multi-Agent capability ≈ $\left(\sum_i$ Agent$_i$ capability$\right)$ × (1 − $\rho_{\text{err}}$)

where $\rho_{\text{err}}$ is the correlation across agents’ error distributions. Two limits clarify what the carriage is for:

  • $\rho_{\text{err}} \to 1$ — perfect correlation. The team behaves as a single agent that costs $N$ times as much. Most naïve multi-agent debates land here, which is consistent with AgentBench’s finding that naïve multi-agent setups can degrade performance by up to 70% on tool-heavy tasks (Section XIII).
  • $\rho_{\text{err}} \to 0$ — the idealised carriage. Errors compound only where they overlap; reliability rises toward $\varepsilon^N$. No production system has reached this regime, but every working multi-agent design is, in effect, pushing toward it.

The formula re-explains earlier sections in a single line. The 70% degradation figure for naïve setups is what high $\rho_{\text{err}}$ looks like. The Retry Storm finding is what high $\rho_{\text{err}}$ looks like under load. The portable-memory work in Section XIII-B is, viewed through this lens, a study of how to keep $\rho_{\text{err}}$ low across projects — a memory format that drags one project’s biases into the next is a decorrelation regression.

What this framework lets us discuss

Three downstream questions become tractable once Carriage is on this footing, and each is a natural successor to questions already raised in Sections XIII and XIII-B:

  1. Measuring $\rho_{\text{err}}$. No public benchmark currently measures error correlation across agents in a multi-agent system. A first cut would sample paired errors across roles on a fixed task and report the conditional miss-rate — a direct empirical handle on whether a carriage is doing its decorrelation job.
  2. Choosing decorrelation knobs. Distinct prompts, distinct skills, distinct backbones, distinct contexts. Each has a cost. The framework asks which knob buys the most decorrelation per unit complexity, and the answer is almost certainly task-dependent.
  3. Carriage portability. Section XIII-B’s open question on project-to-project memory transfer is, in this framework, a question about whether the new project inherits the old project’s carriage or rebuilds it. The decorrelation principle suggests that some carriage state (lessons, dead-end catalogues) should transfer while other state (frozen role beliefs) should not — a sharper design constraint than the one Section XIII-B was able to state on its own.

The Carriage thesis is offered here as a working frame, not a finished theory. It has the right shape: it is symmetric to harness in construction, it has a structural cause (correlated hallucination) rather than only a list of components, and it generates testable predictions. The next sections continue to use the term in its informal sense where the precision is not needed; the formal definition is what we return to whenever a load-bearing claim is being made about multi-agent design.

XIII-BPortable and transferable memory

If a Carriage is the coordination layer that turns agents into a team, then portable memory is what lets that team carry its experience from one project to the next. The field is converging on the problem; no one has solved it.

A research team that forgets everything between projects is not a team — it is a rotating cast of strangers. The same is true for multi-agent systems. MinionsOS’s three-layer memory (Scratchpad / Library / Atlas) solves the within-project problem: roles accumulate and share knowledge across sessions. But the between-project problem — carrying lessons, procedures, and hard-won heuristics from project A to project B — is architecturally unsolved. This section maps the emerging literature on portable, transferable, and pluggable agent memory.

Not all memory is equally portable. The literature reveals a spectrum from maximally portable (plain text files) to maximally coupled (model-internal representations):

Key systems and their portability story

Cross-task experience transfer

SMITH (Liu et al., 2025) unifies dynamic tool creation with cross-task experience sharing through a hierarchical cognitive memory (procedural + semantic + episodic). Achieves 81.8% Pass@1 on GAIA by retrieving successful execution patterns from prior tasks and applying them to novel ones. StackPlanner (Zhang et al., 2026) takes this further with RL-based retrieval of reusable coordination experience across tasks in a multi-agent setting.

Cross-model memory compatibility

WebCoach (Liu et al., 2025) provides the strongest empirical evidence: the same episodic memory store improves performance across three different LLM backbones, with a 38B model plus WebCoach matching GPT-4o without it. A-MEM (Xu et al., 2025) evaluates across six foundation models, showing its Zettelkasten-inspired memory network works regardless of backend. MACLA (Forouzandeh et al., 2025) keeps the LLM frozen and puts all adaptation in external procedural memory — upgrading the model doesn’t invalidate the memory.

Multi-agent memory sharing protocols

Mesh Memory Protocol (Xu, 2026) is the most ambitious: a protocol-level specification for cross-session, cross-agent cognitive collaboration. Four primitives — CAT7 (7-field schema), SVAF (per-field semantic evaluation at acceptance), inter-agent lineage (content-hash provenance), and remix (store only the receiver’s filtered understanding) — address echo chambers, stale retrieval, and field-level admission. Running in production.

Collaborative Memory (Rezazadeh et al., 2025) takes the governance angle: private + shared tiers with bipartite access graphs and immutable provenance. Enables safe cross-user knowledge transfer while enforcing asymmetric permissions.

Pluggable modular architectures

MIRIX (Wang & Chen, 2025) is explicitly designed as a plug-and-play system: six memory components (Core, Episodic, Semantic, Procedural, Resource, Knowledge Vault) managed by a multi-agent architecture. Achieves 35% improvement over RAG while reducing storage 99.9%. MemGPT/Letta (Packer et al., 2023) established the model-agnostic tiered-memory pattern that all subsequent work builds on.

The memory rot problem

Transferring memory is only useful if the memory is still valid. Three 2026 papers directly address this:

“Useful Memories Become Faulty” (Zhang et al., UIUC/Tsinghua, 2026) is the most important negative result: consolidated memories degrade with repeated updates — GPT-5.4 fails on 54% of ARC-AGI problems it previously solved after consolidating from ground-truth solutions. Raw episodic traces are more robust than abstractions. Implication for transfer: transferring consolidated summaries may be worse than transferring raw trajectories.

STALE (Chao et al., 2026) benchmarks whether agents can detect invalidated memories: best model achieves only 55.2% accuracy on 400 expert-validated conflict scenarios. Agents are bad at knowing when their memories are wrong.

FSFM (Gu et al., 2026) and FadeMem (Wei et al., 2026) propose biologically-inspired forgetting: selective decay modulated by relevance, access frequency, and temporal patterns. FadeMem achieves 45% storage reduction while improving multi-hop reasoning. The lesson: deciding what not to carry is as important as what to carry.

The raw-trajectory principle

The memory-rot finding is not just a caveat — it is a design principle, and it inverts a default that has been baked into knowledge-management thinking since long before the agent era. The default assumption is that conclusions are the durable artifact and raw logs are archived for compliance. The Zhang et al. result says the opposite: raw logs are durable, conclusions are not. An ARC-AGI agent given its own consolidated abstractions of prior solutions fails on 54% of problems it had previously solved; the same agent with access to raw solution traces does not exhibit this regression.

Why consolidation degrades is mechanical. An abstraction strips the conditions under which a rule was valid — “use strategy X” loses the implicit “when the input has property Y.” After several rounds of summary-of-summary, the rule remembered has been dissociated from the conditions that made it correct. Each application then activates in cases where the original episode would have been silent. The dual-trace encoding work [Stern & Nadel 2026] gives the positive mirror: every stored fact paired with a “scene trace” — a concrete reconstruction of the encoding context — achieves 73.7% on LongMemEval-S versus 53.5% for fact-only, with the gain concentrated in temporal reasoning (+40pp), knowledge-update tracking (+25pp), and multi-session aggregation (+30pp). The scene trace is the boundary condition that the abstract fact has lost.

The procedural fix has three parts. Episodes are the canonical store — the trajectory record is what is committed and what survives across migrations. Consolidation is single-pass — summaries are derived from raw episodes, never from prior summaries; each round of abstraction runs over the original store, not the previous round’s output. Retrieval favours raw first — when an agent asks “what do I know about X?”, the default return is matching episodes, with summaries kept as a cost-saving fallback for when the matching set is too large to load. RAG-as-retrieval norms run the opposite direction; the principle reverses them.

Case-based, not rubric-based

The raw-trajectory principle has a sibling in the evaluation literature, and the two reinforce each other. Where rubric-based grading asks an LLM to apply abstract criteria (“does this output show good reasoning?”), case-based grading asks the LLM to compare a candidate to a small set of concrete reference examples (“is this more like the good case A or the bad case B?”). The pattern has a long history in case-based reasoning (Schank; Aamodt & Plaza, 1994) and has been recently re-popularised in agent-skill evaluation harnesses, which run a fixed library of behavioural cases rather than scoring against an abstract skill description.

Both moves bet on the same thing: concrete situated knowledge travels better across contexts than abstract general rules, because the situated form preserves the boundary conditions that the abstract form drops. In the evaluation layer this means a judge model walking through cases rather than applying a rubric. In the memory layer it means episodic recall as the default rather than consolidated summary retrieval. The same epistemic logic — that boundary conditions are load-bearing and abstraction is lossy — operates in both.

Implications for cross-project transfer

The principle bites hardest at the cross-project boundary. When a project closes and its memory is to be made available to a future project, the temptation is to compress: produce a concise “lessons learned” document the next team can read in five minutes. The literature suggests this is exactly the wrong move. The compressed lesson is the artifact most likely to mislead, because it has been stripped of precisely the conditions needed to decide whether to apply it.

This reverses the conventional knowledge-management architecture, where wisdom is the durable artifact and raw logs are archived for compliance — but it matches what the rot literature, the dual-trace literature, and the case-based evaluation literature are all separately telling us.

Memory transfer is not the migration of conclusions. It is the migration of evidence.

Security: memory as attack surface

Zombie Agents (Yang et al., 2026) demonstrates that portable memory is a security surface: an attacker can implant a payload that survives across sessions and agent instances by exploiting the normal memory update process. Any cross-project transfer design must treat incoming memory as untrusted input. A security survey (Lin et al., 2026) introduces “mnemonic sovereignty” — verifiable governance over what may be written, read, updated, and forgotten — as the framework for memory lifecycle security.

What the field has not solved

Four gaps remain open as of mid-2026:

1. No memory interchange standard. MMP’s CAT7 schema is the most concrete proposal (7 fields: content, role, timestamp, confidence, lineage, context, hash), but it is a single-vendor spec. There is no equivalent of MCP for memory.

2. No cross-model compatibility characterisation. WebCoach, A-MEM, and Mem0 show empirically that the same store works across backends, but nobody has formally characterised what makes memory model-agnostic vs. model-specific.

3. No project-to-project transfer in multi-agent systems. StackPlanner and SMITH do cross-task transfer within a single system. Nobody has published on carrying lessons from “project A with team X” to “project B with team Y” at the organisational level.

4. No memory adaptation mechanism. MACLA’s Bayesian reliability tracking is the closest, but it operates within a single task distribution. How to adapt memory written for one domain to a new domain without full re-collection is unsolved.

Implication for Carriage design The literature converges on a design principle: memory should be stored at the highest portability level that preserves its utility. Plain text (L1) is maximally portable but loses structure. Protocol-level schemas (L3) preserve structure but require adoption. Role-indexed memory (L4) is the sweet spot for multi-agent systems: it survives model swaps, framework swaps, and even team composition changes, as long as the role contract is stable. This is exactly what MinionsOS’s Library layer provides — and the gap (project-to-project transfer) is exactly what a Carriage-level “memory export/import” primitive would fill.

XIII-CWhen agents themselves should split or merge

A live pilot, run on Haiku 4.5, on whether expert agents should evolve their own population at runtime — splitting one generalist into two specialists when a domain heterogeneity shows up, and merging back when one specialist starves. The mechanism works. The trigger does not fire on tasks a strong generalist can already saturate. This is the inverse of the Carriage thesis at the population layer.

The previous two sections argued that agent ensembles benefit from a coordination Carriage and from portable memory. A third, equally tempting move is to evolve the population itself: an Expert that started life as a generalist “mathematician” should, the intuition goes, notice when its work has drifted into two distinct sub-domains and split into “algebraist” and “geometer.” If a sub-domain dries up later, the two should merge back. This is the population-layer analog of skill discovery: not new procedures, but new role identities. We built it, ran it, and report what actually happened.

The hypothesis the literature has already half-answered

Five recent papers triangulate the question. AgentSpawn (Li et al., 2026) reports +34% completion on SWE-bench-class long-horizon code generation by spawning child agents on runtime complexity signals; the domain matters because cross-file context overflow is the actual failure mode. EvoSkill (2603.02766) moves its specialization budget into the skill layer instead of the agent layer, on the explicit grounds that skill libraries are cheaper to grow and more transferable; +7.3% / +12.1% on two QA benchmarks, with zero-shot skill transfer across tasks. SkillMAS (2605.09341) couples skill evolution and multi-agent restructuring, but only restructures “when retained failures and Executor Utility indicate a structural mismatch” — evidence-gated, not diversity-gated. The self-organisation study (2603.28990) runs a 25,000-task experiment over 8 models and 4–256 agents and finds that capable models spontaneously form roles given just a mission and a fixed ordering protocol — designed roles add little. And Singh et al. (2601.04748) argue that a multi-agent system can usually be compiled into a single agent plus a skill library, with a phase-transition in skill-selection accuracy as the library grows past a critical semantic-confusability threshold. Read together, these point in one direction: error-gated, skill-first specialisation, with population edits reserved for regimes where context isolation actually pays off.

What was missing was a controlled, end-to-end execution of the population-edit primitive itself, with the supervisor making the decision and the worker carrying it out. That is the gap this pilot fills.

The harness

Three policies, all running Haiku 4.5 as both worker and supervisor: static-1 is a single “mathematician” agent; static-N uses 2–3 specialists with an LLM router; dynamic starts as static-1, and after each problem the supervisor reads a window of recent transcripts and may SPLIT the generalist into 2–4 specialists or MERGE two specialists back into a generalist. SPLIT fires only when the window contains both heterogeneity (≥2 tasks of each of ≥2 sub-domains) and at least one error; MERGE fires only when a specialist has received fewer than 2 tasks in the window. The supervisor authors charters and pitfalls per role — it does not pick from a fixed taxonomy.

Six task streams, all graded against verified integer ground truth: a baseline algebra/geometry mix, a depletion-drift variant designed to exercise the MERGE path, a hard variant with multi-step traps, a noisy variant prefixing each problem with off-domain trivia, a cross-frame combinatorics-vs-probability probe with a long preamble, and an ordinal-vs-inventory trap stream. Three seeds per cell where feasible, 33 runs total, ~1.1M input tokens, ~46K output tokens. Code, raw transcripts, and the supervisor's per-decision rationale live in the project repository at experiments/expert_split_merge/.

The harder regime: when does the trigger actually fire?

To test whether the population edits ever pay off, we built a second probe: 30 problems split across combinatorics, probability, and number theory, each prefixed with a 2.2 KB near-noise preamble (semi-relevant math-y prose engineered to bait pattern-completion). At full output budget Haiku still scored 30/30 as a generalist. Constraining the worker to 80 output tokens — a stand-in for situations where reasoning bandwidth is genuinely limited (cost caps, latency budgets, weaker models) — finally produced a regime where static-1 fell to 25/30 (83%). In that regime the supervisor has an error signal to react to. Whether dynamic split actually recovers the lost accuracy is the next experiment, currently in flight; the headline finding so far is that the trigger condition is only met when the worker is operating near, not above, its capability ceiling.

What this means for population-layer evolution

Putting the pilot next to the prior literature gives a coherent picture. Dynamic agent populations earn their keep when one or more of three conditions holds: long-horizon contexts that overflow a single agent's window (AgentSpawn's regime, with cross-file state), heterogeneous infrastructure that is cheaper to maintain per specialist than per generalist (distinct tool allow-lists, sandboxes, rate budgets), and adversarial frames where the split prevents rationalisation (the structural reason MinionsOS keeps Ethics as a separate role). On bounded text-only tasks with a strong generalist, none of these conditions hold, and the literature's other strand — specialisation at the skill layer rather than the agent layer — becomes the more honest path. Skills are cheaper to grow, more transferable across tasks, and selectable through hierarchical routing once the library outgrows a single agent's prompt.

The recommendation that follows is conservative: keep the SPLIT / MERGE primitives available as operator-facing tools gated on observed agent failure logs, never on diversity-of-tasks heuristics; bias the system toward growing per-role skill libraries first; and revisit the population-edit case only when an Expert workload actually fails in a way splitting would fix. SkillMAS-style evidence gating is the right shape. Diversity-driven restructuring is the wrong one.

XIVSeven open questions

Seven concrete problems that inherit directly from Sections III through XIV, and where small teams can still make a first move.

An essay that ends “and now the future is bright” is not worth reading. The six questions below are not speculative; each one is a direct descendant of something left unresolved in Sections III through XIV, and each one is at a stage where evidence is still thin enough that a modest study can move the consensus.

The CUHK-Shenzhen survey gives this agenda a useful external check. Its closing challenges — quality control, interoperability, safe updating, and long-term capability management — are not abstract desiderata here. They translate into concrete tests: whether composed skill stacks outperform monoliths, whether libraries survive language and domain shift, whether artifact protocols remain durable, whether personal skill evolution can be governed, and whether cognitive gates improve scientific outcomes rather than merely improving prose.

Does composition beat specialisation?

Every serious skill collection is built by one author with one style. But nothing prevents a citation skill from one author from being called by a drafting skill from another, as long as both follow the SKILL.md convention. Nobody has measured, with rigour, whether a composed stack outperforms any single monolithic collection on an end-to-end benchmark like PaperBench. This is the cheapest real experiment in the field right now.

Do English benchmarks transfer to other languages?

Every benchmark in Section IV is English-first. GAIA, PaperBench, AutoSurvey, SimpleQA — all of them. There are real reasons to suspect the gap is larger than the language itself: the citation ecology (CNKI and Wanfang rather than Semantic Scholar), the rhetorical conventions of Chinese or Japanese academic prose, the figure caption norms of national journals. A small Chinese-AutoSurvey benchmark, built against real CNKI reference pools rather than GPT-fabricated ones, would be a genuine contribution — and there is no prestigious group obviously working on it yet.

How durable is the ARA protocol?

Section V presented ARA in its best light. The open empirical question is whether the twenty-one-point PaperBench gain survives when you change the backbone model, the agent scaffolding, or the domain. If the gain is robust across all three, ARA is the real thing. If it is not, the intervention is model-specific and the field will keep looking. Either answer is publishable.

What does evolve look like, concretely?

Section VIII closed on the fifth verb without a canonical paper to point to. The open question is not whether personal differentiation of skills is desirable — every working researcher already has a folder of small rewrites of the same template — but what mechanism makes it tractable at scale. A plausible first cut is inheritance: a skill becomes a base record plus a set of per-user patches, each with its own provenance and its own reward history. The test is whether such a library remains consistent for shared tasks while diverging on taste-sensitive ones, and whether two users collaborating on the same project can read one another’s patches without friction.

Do cognitive gates improve discovery, or only prose?

The four-posture story in Section IX is attractive, but attractiveness is not evidence. The next test is whether hard gates reduce measurable failure modes: hidden assumptions, analogy-driven design choices, unresolved tensions, and unclosed feedback loops. If the gains appear only in writing quality or reviewer impression, the framework is rhetoric. If the gains appear in reproducibility, failed-branch reuse, and experiment selection, it becomes infrastructure.

Can an evidence web stay usable after it grows?

Section XI proposes a traceable discovery web, but the hard part is not drawing the graph. It is keeping the graph useful after hundreds of nodes, many agents, and several abandoned branches. A first benchmark should measure receipt completeness, unsupported-claim rate, duplicate-node growth, time-to-audit, and whether negative paths actually prevent repeated failed work. If the web becomes a museum of logs that nobody queries, it fails. If it lets a new agent resume a project without inventing citations, rerunning dead ends, or overstating evidence, it becomes the operating substrate for autonomous science.

Does intra-task learning scale with time?

Section XIII showed that giving agents more time does not reliably improve outcomes. The open question is whether any combination of plan persistence, exploration DAGs, cognitive checkpoints, and dispatch separation can produce a system where performance genuinely scales with allocated time — the way a human PI’s output scales with months of focused attention. If the answer is yes, the mechanism will likely involve structured forgetting (discarding low-value branches) as much as structured remembering. If the answer is no, autonomous science hits a ceiling that no amount of compute can lift, and the field pivots to human-agent collaboration rather than full autonomy.
