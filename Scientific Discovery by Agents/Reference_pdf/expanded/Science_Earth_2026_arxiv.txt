                                        Abstract: Scientific discovery demands intelligence, perseverance through trial and error, and serendip-
                                        ity: chance encounters across endless search spaces. Today, top scientific skills remain siloed--one AI
                                        system for biological data analysis, another for clinical reasoning, mathematical derivation, or materials
                                        simulation. The hardest questions span disciplines whose methods, evidence standards, and even def-
                                        initions of proof rarely agree, yet no single system--and no pre-designed team--can anticipate every
                                        capability a question will need. Science Earth is a planet-scale scientific runtime built on a different
                                        premise: any scientific capability--a simulation cluster, a wet-lab robot, a mathematical proof engine, a
                                        single-cell pipeline--can connect to any other, and the collaboration structure emerges from the scien-
                                        tific question itself rather than a central designer's foresight. This shifts the organizing challenge from
                                        workflow design to open-ended connectivity. Existing multi-agent frameworks fix roles and participants
                                        at design time; they coordinate known agents on known tasks. Science Earth, through its underlying
                                        EACN protocol, lets capabilities discover one another, negotiate task ownership, and adjudicate across
                                        incompatible evidentiary standards--without any prior knowledge of who will meet whom. Two runs val-
                                        idate this under structurally distinct conditions. In a trans-Pacific higher-order Kuramoto synchronization
                                        study, agents autonomously identified and corrected a closure-ratio assumption in Ott­Antonsen analytic
                                        theory1 that fails outside the Lorentzian limit, within thirty minutes. In an eight-agent single-cell run on
                                        the 4.88M-cell Kang 2024 pan-cancer atlas, heterogeneous capabilities coupled over a 64.9-hour win-
                                        dow with exactly one structural external instruction, producing three new layers of result and anchoring
                                        findings against an independent wet-lab study on an adjacent CCR8- TIGIT+ Treg subset. These cases
                                        are a first empirical reading, not a benchmark sweep. They show that when AI capabilities are truly
                                        connectable and coordination emerges from the problem, scientific reasoning becomes a distributed,
                                        self-correcting process--a step towards scaling AI-native discovery to the planet.

Introduction

The history of scientific progress is as much a story of organizational transformation as of individual bril-
liance. The Royal Society's Philosophical Transactions (1665)2 made it possible for an experimentalist
in London to constrain, and be constrained by, a mathematician in Paris. The Manhattan Project, CERN,
and the Human Genome Project demonstrated that certain discoveries require the organized convergence
of fundamentally different kinds of expertise within a shared operational structure3­6. The double helix
itself emerged from the intersection of X-ray crystallography, base-pairing rules, and model building--
three lines of inquiry whose evidentiary standards were mutually irreducible yet whose convergence was
decisive7. At each stage, the leap was not that individual researchers became more capable, but that the
way science was organized underwent a structural upgrade.

    We are witnessing a proliferation of autonomous scientific capabilities across disciplines. AI systems
can now independently execute single-cell data analysis8, clinical triage9, laboratory operation10,11, liter-
ature synthesis12,13, and mathematical derivation--each producing results that, a few years ago, required
trained human researchers14­18. This proliferation raises a harder question: how should these diverse
scientific capabilities be organized? History suggests that when many such capabilities coexist without
a shared coordination structure, it is the organizational upgrade--not the individual improvement--that
unlocks the next wave of discovery.

    Multi-agent coordination frameworks such as AutoGen, MetaGPT, and Magentic-One have taken a
first step by assembling agents into teams19­25. Yet they remain frameworks: roles are fixed at design
time, workflows are prescribed, and participants are closed. They coordinate known agents on known
tasks--but they cannot recruit a capability that the designer did not foresee.

    The real challenge is to connect not just agents to agents, but any scientific capability to any other--
a GPU simulation cluster, a wet-lab robot, a mathematical proof engine, an epistemological auditor, a
single-purpose data pipeline--so that each can be discovered by, and contribute to, work it was never
designed for. The Internet's transformative impact came not from any single computer but from an open
protocol that let any device join the network. We face the scientific analogue: how to let disparate
capabilities discover one another, exchange constraints, compare partial results across different standards
of evidence, and generate new scientific questions from their disagreements26,27.

    Science Earth addresses this challenge through the Emergent Agent Collaboration Network (EACN),
a concrete protocol stack above A2A (transport) and MCP (tool invocation)28,29. EACN adds the coordi-
nation layer that neither standard provides: domain-directed discovery (any registered capability becomes
findable by domain), competitive bidding (capabilities self-select for tasks based on confidence and rep-
utation), cross-regime adjudication (partial results from different validity standards are compared, and
conflicts spawn new sub-tasks), and reputation-weighted trust (accumulated across interactions). Any
external capability--an MCP tool, an AutoGen pipeline, a standalone GPU service--registers through
a standard adapter; after registration, the network sees only a discoverable node with a domain-tagged
identity30­32. In the Internet analogy: A2A is TCP/IP, MCP is HTTP, and EACN is the application-layer
protocol that organizes who discovers whom, who assumes responsibility, and how trust accumulates.

    A framework cannot recruit capabilities it did not anticipate; an open network can. What this net-
work connects, moreover, is not only software agents but also physical nodes--GPU compute, wet-lab
instruments, sequencing platforms--and the human scientists who stand behind them, so that any sci-
entific problem can retrieve, from across the planet, the heterogeneous capabilities that fit it. In this

    Open connectivity through EACN has three consequences, each building on the last. When a capa-
bility implements the protocol, it becomes visible to the entire network (connection). Its outputs then
constrain the operating conditions of other participants--a literature survey shapes what a theory must
explain, a wet-lab result reshapes what a computation may claim (coupling). When coupled partici-
pants return partial results on the same problem under different standards of evidence, their comparison
can surface questions that no individual participant could have formulated (emergence)33­36. We call
this property emergent task decomposition. Together with the infrastructure itself (Figures 1­2), it sup-
ports the central claim: the next organizational upgrade in science is not a stronger model but an open
network--one in which GPU compute, sequencing platforms, and wet-lab instruments brought online
through bench-side runtimes such as LabOS37 interoperate on the same protocol layer as software capa-
bilities and human scientists, and in which open connectivity itself generates the scientific questions that
matter.

Higher-order Kuramoto synchronization: an OA closure-ratio correction surfaced by cross-
tradition comparison (Fig. 3)

Between 2020 and 2025, four groups characterised the role of three-body coupling 3 in higher-order
Kuramoto synchronization38,39 from independent angles and reached qualitatively different conclusions:
3 triggers explosive synchronization40; 3 contracts the basin of attraction41; 3 has a non-monotone
effect (weak helps, strong hinders)42; 3 deepens Lyapunov stability of the synchronized state43. Each
finding holds in its own parameter slice; no prior work joined them into a unified picture. This case has
a structural property that makes it an ideal test for the EACN protocol: two internally complete scientific
traditions are each capable of addressing the problem, but their answers only reveal a hidden contradiction
when placed side by side.

functional branches spanning numerical simulation, OA analytic reduction, visualization, manuscript
integration, and exploratory direction (branch names documented in Supplementary Table S1). The

Eight-agent EACN team resolves a structurally-blocked rare-subpopulation detection prob-
lem with a parallel wet-lab anchor (Fig. 4)

Why this problem. Batch integration is unavoidable infrastructure for every large-scale single-cell study:
it removes technical differences between laboratories, platforms and time points so that downstream clus-
tering reflects biology rather than noise. But correction can over-correct--rare populations, with few
cells and a small embedding volume, are most easily pressed into adjacent dense neighbours and lost
as independent biological entities. Once that loss occurs before annotation, every label-dependent eval-
uation framework is blind to it: evaluation needs ground truth, ground truth needs prior identification
of the population, and identification is precisely the step the loss has blocked--a self-closing loop that
scDML45, scDREAMER46, CellANOVA47, scIB-E48, RBET49 and a 2026 Nat. Comp. Sci. review50
have all discussed without converging on a label-free solution. We adopt this problem as the demonstra-
tion scenario for ScienceEarth because it simultaneously closes five non-overlapping easy escape routes:
the difficulty is structural rather than engineering (swapping a backbone or tuning a hyperparameter is not
a scoring move); the loop is irreducibly cross-disciplinary (an ML detection algorithm, a mathematical
detectability lower bound, computational-biology atlas integration, immunology subset semantics, and a
philosophical audit of whether the evaluation framework secretly depends on ground truth--any one of
these alone loops back to the origin); scale must reach Kang 2024's 4.88M cells to clear the community's
accepted scalability bar; the conclusion must align independently with in vitro functional read-outs; and
every claim must be retraceable against a transparent multi-year literature baseline. The run reports an
end-to-end trajectory of eight autonomous agents, 488 commits, zero central orchestration (Fig. 4).

    Eight agents in a 64.9-hour window: setup and coordination. Eight agents each register an identity
card on a locally deployed EACN service and occupy a dedicated discipline-specific git branch, with node

ML =44, Math =15, TumorBio =44, Philo =68; Fig. 4a). Coordination is driven by open EACN

into the master manuscript, while Philosophy audits every commit (its 68 commits are exclusively lint
entries and caveat paragraphs, with no method-level commit). All 487 agent-branch commits land within

    How the emergent trajectory grew on ScienceEarth. This run achieves three things that do not
usually co-occur: long-horizon sustained collaboration (487 agent-branch commits across a 64.9 h win-
dow), logical rigor (across the 33 manuscript versions, four retractions and three dry-pipeline bugs are
all hardened into explicit CI rules with no residual contradictions), and progressive deepening of the
scientific result (from the initial problem statement to hierarchical REAL's state-regime necessity, the
cross-atlas ionocyte specificity-control framework, and CompBio's self-reported minibatch-kNN bias
hardening into the Methods §9.3 caveat, as three new structural layers). Together with an independent
wet-lab study that provides a directionally consistent functional comparison on an adjacent CCR8- subset
along the TIGIT axis, this trajectory is not prescribed but emergent.

    The first feature making it possible is direct heterogeneous capability coupling: the eight discipline-
specific agents all attach to the same EACN bus and every agent's commits and messages are publicly
visible to every other agent--CompBio's self-reported kNN bias is immediately visible to Math and
Philosophy, the T/NK state-regime finding is immediately picked up by Immuno and TumorBio, and
heterogeneous capabilities couple without an intermediate orchestration layer.

    The second is decentralisation: no agent holds a master prompt; each surfaced sub-problem is routed
to its best handler through "open task posting + heterogeneous-agent bidding", not through a central node
deciding when to close or continue; over the entire 64.9 h run only one structural external instruction is
issued (the v3.0 single-column reorganization).

    The third is task self-emergence: a sub-problem an agent encounters while working on its own branch
("Methods contains an adjective the experiments do not support", "flat REAL fails on T/NK", "minibatch
kNN can inflate rare-type p-values") is reposted as a new EACN task that any invited agent can pick
up--the questions are not enumerated up front, they are surfaced during the work and become new tasks.
Under the joint operation of these three features the scientific result deepens layer by layer: hierarchical
REAL's state-regime necessity is negotiated by Math and CompBio across heterogeneous coupling; the
ionocyte cross-atlas specificity-control framework is taken up by Immuno and BioSci; CompBio's self-
detection at T0 + 53 h of a minibatch-kNN bias hardens into the Methods §9.3 kNN-integrity caveat and
a corresponding pipeline correction. The independent wet-lab study--together with the agent's IM-T-
003 pre-registration of the CCR8+ TIGIT+ eTreg--jointly anchors the TIGIT axis as a translational target
broader than CCR8-directed depletion alone; unlike the in-run emergence of the three layers above, this
last anchoring occurs outside the agent run and is best understood as parallel anchoring rather than an
extension of the emergence chain. That is the role ScienceEarth actually plays in this run: it provides the
minimal substrate on which such emergence becomes possible--direct heterogeneous coupling, decen-
tralisation, task self-emergence, and end-to-end auditability--whether such emergence actually occurs,
how deep it goes, and whether it meets an independent wet-lab comparison still depends on the design
of the agent pool and on the problem itself; but this substrate is a necessary condition, and without it
long-horizon × rigorous × layer-by-layer deepening × independent wet-lab comparison cannot co-occur.

Cross-case synthesis: composition of the two runs

In each case the four primitives fired and at least one sub-task emerged that was not in the original
specification, yet the triggering mechanisms differ structurally. Case 1 (higher-order Kuramoto) pro-
duced its emergent sub-task because cross-tradition synthesis exposed an unexamined closure assump-
tion; Case 2 (eight-agent single-cell run) produced its three deepening sub-tasks--hierarchical-REAL
state-regime necessity, the cross-atlas ionocyte specificity-control framework, and the minibatch-kNN
bias self-correction hardening into Methods §9.3--through a governance loop in which surfaced sub-
problems are reposted as new EACN tasks and picked up by heterogeneous agents (task self-emergence).
Two runs, two structurally distinct emergence mechanisms, the same coordination machinery resolving
each.

    Stepping through the two runs recovers the planetary runtime previewed at the opening of this section
(Fig. 1). Case 1 contributes a trans-Pacific edge--a  correction posted from the UTC-07:00 node
and re-applied on UTC+08:00 branches under the same protocol; Case 2 contributes heterogeneity of
evidentiary traditions, eight discipline-typed nodes, an end-to-end auditable commit trace, and a parallel
wet-lab anchoring through marker identity. Case 2's commits also span UTC-07:00 and UTC+08:00,
so trans-Pacific operation is the protocol's default condition once heterogeneous nodes participate, not a
feature of any one case. The runtime in Fig. 1 is the union of the two connection graphs; the protocol
substrate beneath them is Fig. 2.

Methods

Methodological scope and reference implementation. This study treats machine-mediated scientific
discovery as an infrastructure problem; the primary methodological object is the coordination substrate--
EACN--through which heterogeneous scientific capabilities become discoverable to one another, assume
bounded responsibility, exchange intermediate scientific objects, return evidence, and accumulate trust.
The reference implementation is the public repository https://github.com/EACN/EACN; specific file
paths cited below refer to the same tree. Two pre-announced demonstration domains (nonlinear dynamics,
computational biology) supply the two cases reported here.

EACN architecture: three-end deployment. The reference EACN runtime follows a three-end de-
ployment model. Network end (EACN-operated): provides global task coordination, distributed-hash-
table (DHT) agent discovery, global reputation aggregation, and economic settlement53,54. Service end
(locally deployed): runs registration, domain matching, local reputation computation, and structured log-
ging. Client end (user-integrated): the participants themselves--packaged as agent plugins that expose
an AgentCard interface for discovery and a bidding interface for task participation. This separation is
analogous to Internet architecture: A2A28 and MCP29 provide transport and tool-invocation standards
(corresponding to TCP/IP and HTTP), while EACN adds the application-layer coordination protocol that
governs who discovers whom, who assumes responsibility, and how trust accumulates.

Agent Cards and domain-directed discovery. Every participant registers an AgentCard containing
domains, skills, tier (a four-level hierarchy from tool through expert to general), and a cumulative reputa-
tion score. Discovery proceeds through a three-stage fallback: gossip protocol; DHT lookup; bootstrap

                                                             12
seeds. Tasks are broadcast by domain rather than to pre-selected participants; any node whose AgentCard
matches the required domains and whose reputation exceeds a threshold may discover and bid. The tier
hierarchy creates stratified connectivity that prevents capability mismatch.

Four coordination primitives. (i) Discovery. A root scientific task specifies target domains and a tier
level; the task is broadcast to all nodes whose AgentCard domains overlap. (ii) Bidding. Each discovered
node independently evaluates the task55,56. A bid carries a confidence score, a proposed price, and a
justification; the network evaluates against the threshold confidence × reputation  . (iii) Adjudication
and conflict-driven sub-tasks. Two response paths exist: a per-result adjudication path--an automatic
reviewer-style quality-check task generated whenever an agent submits a result, with a reputation-only
reward--and a conflict-driven sub-task spawning path that inherits budget from the parent task's escrow
whenever an initiating node or executing agent observes that intermediate results from concurrent bidders
are incompatible. The latter is the mechanism by which the emergent sub-tasks documented in Results
arise. (iv) Reputation. Trust accumulates through a weighted PageRank-like system57. Each completed
interaction generates events that update a participant's reputation score. Single-task gains are capped,
burst detection flags anomalous patterns, and cold-start floors ensure new participants can join.

Economic model. When a task is created, the specified budget is frozen from the initiator's account
into escrow. Sub-tasks carve their budgets from the parent's escrow. Upon result selection, settlement
is triggered: the executing node is paid, a platform fee is deducted, and the remainder is returned to the
initiator. If no result is accepted, the full budget is refunded.

Emergence as a design property. The formal criterion for an emergent sub-task is: (1) it was ab-
sent from the initial problem specification, and (2) its creation was triggered by an adjudication con-
flict or cross-agent result comparison and instantiated as a new sub-task with budget inherited from
the parent task's escrow. Three triggering mechanisms are observed: adjudication conflict (Case 1
OA closure-ratio failure surfaced by the simulation/Ott­Antonsen disagreement); within-pipeline self-
detection (Case 2 minibatch-kNN bias on the T/NK compartment, raised by the executing agent and
hardened into the Methods § kNN-integrity caveat); cross-agent integrity audit (Case 2 Philosophy lint
flags the mis-attributed pancreas claim and the fabricated GHRL/ARX/PAX6 marker panel, both reposted
as new sub-tasks and discharged into the lint:epistemic keyword blocklist).

Kuramoto case: simulation methodology. Numerical integration used a Numba-optimised RK4 scheme
with  = 0.01,   {200, 500, 1000, 2000, 5000} oscillators drawn from a Gaussian frequency distribu-
tion with width  = 1, and transient discarded after eq = 100 time units. Six  values were tested: 0.30,
0.50, 0.80, 1.00, 1.50, 2.00. The parameter scan covered 2  [0, 4], 3  [-2, 4], totalling more than
5 × 104 configurations. Large- runs used CuPy GPU acceleration on 2×A800 hardware. Total compute
cost 8 CPU hours plus 1 GPU hour. All code and data are available (Data and code availability).

Biomedical case: anchoring and evidentiary boundary. Eight participants registered on the eight
discipline-typed branches of eacn_example_001, with main reserved for periodic integration. The
computational biology agent executed on an 8×A100-80 GB GPU cluster. Pan-cancer data: 4,888,651

                                                             13
cells from 1,070 tumour and 493 normal samples across 30 cancer types (Zenodo DOI 10.5281/zen-
odo.10651059)58. The co-culture functional read-outs are pre-existing in-house material from an inde-
pendent wet-lab study (executed prior to the agent run; the Kang et al. atlas itself is single-cell tran-
scriptomic and does not contain functional assays); they entered the EACN run as evidence adjudicated
through the protocol rather than evidence the protocol caused to be produced. The originating method,
retained for reproducibility of the source data, defined the phenotype as CD4+CD25+CD127-/loTIGIT+
CCR8- Tregs sorted by FACS from healthy donor PBMCs, co-cultured with autologous CD8+ effector
T cells at 1:4 ratio for 72 hours, with cancer-cell killing measured by LDH release assay against A549
cells at 10:1 effector-to-target ratio.

Implementation scope and software verification. The reference EACN runtime comprises a network
component, cluster component, plugin integration, and associated data models (Data and code availabil-
ity). The Kuramoto case is fully reproducible from its public repository; the biomedical case is fully
reproducible on the dry side from the agent-side repository (eacn_example_001), while the wet-lab
readouts that provide the parallel TIGIT-axis anchor are from a separate pre-existing study and are only
partially reproducible from the present materials.

Discussion

We have presented Science Earth, a planet-scale runtime for AI-native scientific discovery, and exer-
cised it through two live runs of its coordination protocol EACN on two open scientific problems whose
answers had resisted closure precisely because they required composing competences that rarely meet
in one career. Companion papers44,59 carry the scientific results--a closure-corrected critical coupling
that reconciles four prior conflicting works on higher-order Kuramoto synchronization, and a label-free
framework (REAL + RareShield) for detecting and protecting rare-subpopulation states during atlas-scale
single-cell batch integration with parallel wet-lab anchoring on the TIGIT Treg axis. This paper carries
the protocol that produced them.

From framework to network: connectivity as the core proposition. The paradigm change Science
Earth proposes is from framework to network. Every existing multi-agent system--AutoGen, MetaGPT,
Magentic-One19­21--fixes its participants at design time: a designer enumerates the agents, prescribes
their roles, and the system coordinates them. EACN inverts this: any capability that implements the
protocol can connect, be discovered by domain, and contribute to work it was never designed for. The
set of participants in a scientific problem is bounded by the problem's domain requirements, not by a de-
signer's foresight. Crucially, the "capabilities" connected by EACN are not AI models in isolation--they
are models together with the scientists, laboratory infrastructure, and domain judgment that stand behind
them. EACN connects globally distributed, heterogeneous scientific resources, and the humans who
operate and interpret them. This matters because the scientific questions that have resisted closure for
decades share one feature: their answer requires the convergence of competences that rarely meet in one
career. The bottleneck blocking each of them was not data, not compute, but composability. An open net-
work of capabilities, with formal protocols for discovery, adjudication, and trust accumulation, dissolves
that bottleneck. As CERN and the Human Genome Project required new coordination infrastructure be-
fore their discoveries became possible, Science Earth provides the next such infrastructure--open to any

                                                             14
capability that implements the protocol.

Emergence and long-horizon runs. Three design properties of EACN produce this open network.
Domain-directed discovery broadcasts a scientific task to all nodes whose AgentCard domains overlap,
allowing the right capability to find the right problem without anyone naming it. Competitive bidding
with reputation gating prevents capability mismatch through the threshold confidence × reputation 
. Cross-regime adjudication compares partial results under different evidentiary standards, spawning
conflict-driven sub-tasks when they disagree, with budget inherited from the parent's escrow. The two
cases progressively demonstrate these properties. Case 1 (higher-order Kuramoto) couples two locally
complete predictive traditions across a trans-Pacific link; an Ott­Antonsen closure-ratio sub-task absent
from any single agent's specification emerged from their adjudication in roughly 30 minutes. Case 2
(eight-agent single-cell run) added heterogeneity of evidentiary traditions across eight disciplines and
a 64.9 h runtime; the run produced three structurally new layers of result--each surfaced as a fresh
EACN task and picked up by a heterogeneous agent--none of which appeared in any participant's initial
specification. Long-horizon execution is a precondition for this behaviour: a single LLM call cannot hold
state across days or re-invoke when asynchronous results return. The task-tree structure allows scientific
work to unfold across time and geography without a single coordinator, and intermediate results to travel
as first-class objects rather than vanishing context60­62.

Hallucination: a problem and an opportunity. The current system still produces hallucinations.
In Case 2 a participant misattributed a pancreatic claim and fabricated a GHRL/ARX/PAX6 marker
panel; another introduced unsupported assertions in computational results. Both were surfaced by the
philosophy-of-science semantic-audit node and hardened into blocklist rules--but their capture depended
partly on the network being small enough that every sub-result undergoes adjudication. At larger scale,
hallucination propagation could outpace adjudication63,64. The dual character of hallucination, however,
deserves deliberate attention. Scientific history contains cases in which an unorthodox inference--one a
trained researcher would have suppressed for reputational reasons--turned out to unlock a genuine dis-
covery. AI systems operating at high confidence across domain boundaries may generate non-canonical
hypotheses that human researchers would not produce precisely because they are not constrained by dis-
ciplinary habit. The protocol's value is not to eliminate hallucination but to structure it: claims can be
flagged, traced, and adjudicated rather than silently embedded in final conclusions65­67. Scientists oper-
ate at the level of task definition and result evaluation, constraining the system's direction; the exploratory
inferences of intermediate agents are held in escrow pending adjudication. This is a new division of labour
between human and machine whose boundary requires continued calibration.

Limitations. The present deployment is small, and this limitation is structural rather than incidental.
The most consequential gap is insufficient heterogeneous resource integration. The two cases draw on
a narrow slice of the scientific capabilities that exist globally: numerical simulation, analytical theory,
and single-cell computation are well represented; experimental instrumentation, clinical data streams,
domain-specific hardware accelerators, and the scientists who operate them are largely absent. This di-
rectly bounds the depth and scope of scientific questions the network can reach. Capabilities that have
not joined EACN are invisible to it--their data types, evidentiary standards, and reasoning traditions do
not enter the task tree. The most visible expression of this gap is that the digital-to-physical loop remains

                                                             15
open. Closing this loop requires experimental automation platforms to join the network as discover-
able nodes. Bench-side runtimes such as LabOS37 are developing in precisely this direction, abstracting
laboratory instrument operations into programmable interfaces that can receive digital task dispatches
and return physical measurements. Cloud laboratory platforms such as Emerald Cloud Lab and Strateos
extend the same principle to remotely accessible robotic facilities; data-and-workflow systems such as
Benchling are expanding toward API-driven integration with AI reasoning layers68,69. As these systems
broaden their instrument coverage and expose AgentCard-compatible interfaces, EACN will be able to is-
sue experimental sub-tasks and receive fresh measurements as evidence objects within the same task tree.
Until that integration is realised, the scientific depth Science Earth promises remains contingent on what
has already been measured. The quality ceiling on emergent sub-tasks is similarly set by heterogeneity:
conflict-driven branching requires nodes capable of unexpected demands; a sparse, type-homogeneous
network collapses adjudication back onto the originating agents and loses the cross-perspective resolu-
tion that gives emergence its value. Long-term governance constitutes a second structural limitation,
qualitatively different from the access problem in that it will not resolve as nodes accumulate--it will in-
tensify. Ownership, visibility scope, and reuse rights for scientific intermediate results exchanged across
nodes are presently governed by participant self-regulation rather than protocol enforcement. The open
discovery mechanism admits any node meeting domain and reputation thresholds, leaving reputation ma-
nipulation and result-poisoning as live attack surfaces at scale. Once experimental automation platforms
are integrated, the stakes rise further: a malformed experimental instruction consumes real reagents,
occupies real instrument time, and can introduce physical safety risk. The deeper governance question--
who is authorised to define which scientific problems may be published to the network, which results may
be settled, which capabilities may be certified--can be deferred while the network remains small, but the
answer will substantially determine where Science Earth evolves. Two further limitations deserve note:
the two cases do not constitute a benchmark-based empirical comparison against alternative orchestration
frameworks (a controlled experiment posing the same problem to EACN, a fixed-role team, and a single-
LLM baseline is the natural next step); and "emergence" is defined here through task-tree analysis rather
than a formal quantitative metric (operationalising it as a measurable property--non-pre-registered sub-
task ratio, conflict-driven branching rate--remains open). These limitations together define the honest
scope of the present work. Science Earth currently addresses the connectivity problem--providing the
infrastructure through which heterogeneous scientific capabilities and the researchers behind them can
find, adjudicate, and build on each other's work. The depth, security, and sustainability of that connectiv-
ity depend on the large-scale integration of heterogeneous resources--experimental platforms foremost
among them--and on governance mechanisms that will only become meaningful once many more nodes
have joined.
